A Childhood Spent in the Little Maids’ Room

In that cavernous and opulent mansion of my youth, a labyrinth of gilded corridors and vaulted chambers that echoed with the cold grandeur of my family’s station, there existed, tucked like an afterthought beneath the servants’ stair, a cramped and dimly lit room, spartan in its furnishings and unadorned by the pretensions of luxury, wherein I, paradoxically, discovered the purest semblance of freedom. It was there, amidst the hushed whispers and weary sighs of those who laboured invisibly to sustain our household’s splendor, that I found myself unshackled from the stifling decorum of my birthright. There, in the company of maids whose hands were calloused from work yet whose laughter flowed as freely as the monsoon rains, that we would while away the hours in conspiratorial gossip regarding my parents’ stern proclivities and my siblings’ frivolities, or lose ourselves in the tinny melodies of foreign radios, or endure, with equal parts mockery and delight, the garish theatrics of second-rate Bollywood films projected upon a flickering screen, or huddle over checkered game boards until our eyes grew heavy and sleep draped itself over us like a threadbare shawl. 

 

These women—displaced, disenfranchised, their Arabic fractured into the utilitarian pidgin of the Gulf—became, in those formative years, my most steadfast confidantes; and though the barriers of language might have rendered our discourse clumsy to an outsider’s ear, we forged between us a lexicon of gesture and inflection, of half-words and knowing silences, through which they divulged their sorrows and I, in turn, presumed to offer counsel, and all the while that my childish audacity was met never met with condescension but with a weary gratitude that acknowledged, perhaps, the novelty of being heard.

 

What tutelage I received within those close, shadowed walls. From earliest boyhood until the cusp of my fifteenth year, I harboured an insatiable hunger for the fragments of their lives they deigned to share: the faded photographs of crumbling ancestral homes, pressed into my palms like sacred relics; the ballads crooned in languages unknown to me, their melodies saturated with the ache of exile; the tales of perilous travels undertaken across seas and deserts, all to secure for distant children—whose faces they kissed only in dreams—a pittance of wages from the hands of those who regarded them as phantoms, transient and unbelonging. How keenly I sensed, even then, that I served as proxy for the sons and daughters they had left behind in dusty, sun-scorched villages! These women—whose existence was reduced to waiting in that airless room for the shrill summons of a bell; waiting through the interminable years for reunion with families grown strange; waiting, always, for some reprieve from the thousand indignities of servitude—bestowed upon me, in their loneliness, a gift far greater than the scattered phrases of Malayalam or Tagalog I would later forget, viz— the revelation that kinship, as much as metcy, flourishes most vividly in the cracks between power and powerlessness, and that the heart, when attuned to the whispers of the marginalised, may yet learn to beat in time with the rhythms of a wider, wounded world.

 

It was through these fragmented confessions that I grew intimate with the spectres of their hunger, the gnawing voids of their homelands’ poverty, and the cruel paradox that bound them: that in fleeing desperation, they had collided with a world whose laws, leaden and unyielding, conspired to fix them in that airless purgatory beneath the stairs, their days a Sisyphean procession of trifling tasks, their nights haunted by the phantasmal laughter of children whose voices grew fainter with each passing year. Out of crippling boredom, even the most trifle things would amuse them just as they would children; I recall the infantile gaiety in their faces when they were allowed to accompany me to a theme park or a children’ party or whatever event outside of that sulky room which I so adored.

 

And yet—how bitterly ironic!—it was the servants, not the masters, who stood accused of depravity. The parlours and diwans of my family’s peers resounded with lurid tales of their savagery: of maids who pilfered jewellery to fund clandestine orgies; of drivers who seduced virginal daughters with pagan incantations; of cooks who laced biryanis with love potions to bewitch cuckolded husbands; of nannies who whispered heresies into the ears of pliant infants, corrupting their ‘pure’ Arabic with the barbarous cadences of Kerala or Kathmandu. Such calumnies, dripping from the lips of men who thought nothing of backhanding a gardener for a smudged vase or cornering a housemaid in a linen closet, struck me even then as a grotesque distortion, most likely a projection of the nobility’s own rot onto those too powerless to refute it. For who, truly, were the thieves? The men who seized passports as collateral, who docked wages to settle imaginary debts, who treated their staff as chattel to be traded or discarded at whim. Who were the defilers? The uncles and cousins who slunk into servants’ quarters under cover of night, their intentions as transparent as they were vile. Who were the heathens? Those who knelt in ostentatious piety at dawn, only to spend their evenings scheming over whiskey and water pipes and their morality as flimsy as the gold leaf adorning their ceilings? Nevertheless, my friends—for so I still dared to call them, though the gulf of privilege yawned between us—bore these slanders with a resignation that suggested centuries of practice.

 

And thus, by degrees imperceptible, my affinity for those exiled souls deepened into a kinship that transcended the crude taxonomies of blood and station as new bonds were drawn from my cousins’ derisive incredulity whenever they discovered me nestled among the maids, and only a sharper awareness of the chasm between their complacent prejudices and my own unorthodox allegiances. To them, my predilection for the company of the subaltern was not merely eccentric but tainted by a suspect softness, a failure of masculine rigour; “Why do you squat with the help like some scullery urchin?” they would sneer, their voices laced with that peculiar blend of mockery and unease that attends all transgressions of unspoken codes. That association with servants was inherently degrading, that their presence was a miasma capable of corroding the moral fibre of their betters. These were articles of faith in our milieu and required no more examination than the air we breathed; to question such axioms was to court ridicule and a subtler, more pernicious ostracism, the quiet violence of a gaze that marks one as contaminated. Yet in their taunts I detected, beneath the performative scorn, a tremor of fear, perhaps that of the porousness of boundaries they believed to be so immutable, that the myths sustaining our privilege, the fables of inherent superiority, of servants as vectors of corruption, might very well crumble if subjected to the scrutiny of a child’s unclouded eyes.

Among those luminous outcasts, none shone more brightly than Suwaida—she of the calloused hands and bottomless laugh, who arrived at our estate in the autumn of 1997, a yearling babe myself, and remained, through the slow unraveling of decades, both my clandestine solace and the living rebuttal of every slander levied against her caste. Her origins, as she recounted them during stolen hours in the maids’ room, were the stuff of epic lament: a hamlet in the Bengali hinterlands where the soil itself seemed to weep with the futility of cultivation; a girlhood measured in skipped meals and siblings sold into early marriages; an indentured trip to Qatar that she narrated not at all as tragedy but triumph, for the pittance she earned in our employ became the remittance-laden lifeline that spared her own children from the maw of the same hunger. Oh how fiercely she loved them! How she hoarded Polaroids of their gap-toothed grins beneath her thin mattress, how her voice caught on the vowels of their names—Amit, Priya—as if speaking them aloud might conjure their ghosts into that joyless room! And though my parents, vigilant sentinels of propriety, insisted that Suwaida remain forever staff, a functionary whose humanity must be bracketed by the parentheses of her duties, I flouted their decrees with the serene defiance of one who knows, in her marrow, that love recognises no titles. To me, she was neither maid nor martyr but something akin to an errant aunt, a co-conspirator against the adults who smuggled sweets into my schoolbag, who hummed Rabindra Sangeet as she played with my hair, who schooled me, through her example in the art of finding dignity amid indignity.

 

My mother, of course, viewed this intimacy as a species of mutiny and suspicion. “You mustn’t blur the lines,” she would admonish, her voice taut with the anxiety of one who intuits, however dimly, that the entire edifice of her world rests upon the fiction of those lines’ inviolability. With the desperation of a general fortifying a besieged citadel, she devised ever-stricter protocols to reinscribe the hierarchy my heart had dissolved: servants were forbidden to eat at our table, to address us by name without honourifics, to linger in rooms unsummoned. Yet what were her decrees against the gravitational pull of Suwaida’s warmth? Against the nights when I’d creep downstairs, blanket in hand, to find her weeping over a letter from Priya, and we’d sit knee-to-knee on the cold tile floor, parsing the smudged Bengali script by flashlight? The boundaries she sought to impose were, in the end, as porous as the borders of empires, and just as prone to rebellion by those who refuse to know their place.

 

It is a truth universally unacknowledged, yet etched into the marrow of every household, that the bond between child and servant is a fragile heaven because it is unspoiled until the serpent of adult prejudice slithers in to decree its corruption. Children detect instinctively the artifice of hierarchy; they recoil from the performative sternness of elders, whose benevolence is so often laced with condescension, whose affection is doled out like alms to the undeserving. To the child, the servant is neither inferior nor instrument but simply person, a fellow conspirator in the subversion of dreary propriety by the adults. Thus did I, in my youth, gravitate toward the maids’ room by the stifling air of their performative adulthood, where laughter was measured and play staged as pedagogy. Our house visitors, with their saccharine cooing and laboured attempts at camaraderie, inevitably betrayed their ulterior motive: to educate, to mold, to rehearse the tired script of condesncening generational authority. But the servants played as though play were a sacred rite and their mirth was untainted by the need to instruct or improve. In their company, I was not a project to be perfected but another person to be met as an equal; and when the games concluded, we would sit cross-legged on the floor, allies in silent rebellion, rolling our eyes at the shared absurdity of grown-ups who fancied themselves sovereigns of a world they scarcely understood.

 

It happened that this diffidence, which adults dismissed as timidity or defect, became, in the shadowed alcove of the maids’ room, a kind of passport, a guarantee of goodwill. From earliest memory, I harboured a terror of human scrutiny so acute it bordered on the somatic; to enter a room teeming with guests was to endure a trial by fire, my shoulders hunched like a turtle retreating into its shell, my gaze fixed resolutely on the floor as if the very pattern of the tiles might offer sanctuary. This trembling aversion to society, which my family deemed such a mortifying eccentricity, endeared me to the servants, who perhaps after all recognised in my reticence neither weakness nor disdain but a reflection of their own enforced invisibility. Unlike the swaggering cousins who barked orders or the aunts who critiqued their scrubbing technique with imperial fastidiousness, I could not muster the same arrogance to command—could not, in truth, meet their eyes without flushing crimson. What they made of this quivering boy-child, I cannot say; only that they responded not with the robotic deference reserved for others but with a tenderness that seemed to say, We see you. We, too, have known the weight of existing as ghosts.

 

And so it was that I, the perennial wallflower, became the unlikely darling of the serving class, not only within our household but in the homes of relatives and neighbours, where maids would beckon me into their dim quarters with conspiratorial smiles, pressing into my palms sticky sweets wrapped in newspaper or trinkets fashioned from scavenged thread. Their devotion, I hasten to clarify, bore no resemblance to the cringing obsequiousness my caste mistook for loyalty; it was, rather, the solidarity of the marginalised, a silent pact between those who dwell on the periphery, who speak in glances and gestures, who understand that kindness, in a world built on cruelty, is both rebellion and sacrament. That I, a son of the oppressor class, should be enfolded into this fraternity of the dispossessed remains a paradox I have yet to unravel; perhaps they glimpsed, beneath my privilege, a kindred fragility—a boy so allergic to the performative brutality of manhood that he, too, might one day be cast out. Or perhaps it was simpler: that in a world where power demanded the constant humiliation of the vulnerable, my meekness was read as peace offering, a white flag raised in the name of our shared humanity.

 

That sanctuary of shared frailty could not, alas, endure forever. The world, with its tyrannies and tribalisms, is not fashioned to permit such heresies as unconditional kinship across the gulf of caste; and so it was that Suwaida, whose laughter had once been my compass in the labyrinth of childhood, became a casualty of that same world’s pitiless arithmetic. I recall, with a vividness that still scalds, the afternoon my cousins cornered her in the pantry, their voices dripping with the venomous glee of those who wield cruelty as birthright—witch, they hissed, negress, kallu—epithets meant to reduce her humanity to caricature. Her face in that moment, a mask of quiet devastation; mine, I suspect, a mirror of impotent rage. To intervene would have branded me traitor to my blood; to remain silent, accomplice to their savagery. And so we stood, she and I, locked in a tableau of complicity: she, forbidden by the precariousness of her station to protest; I, paralysed by the cowardice that masquerades as self-preservation. “You are good,” she would murmur afterward, her voice a frayed thread of solace, “not like them.” How bitterly that absolution rang in my ears! For what goodness resides in mute witness? What virtue in a solidarity that evaporates at the first chill of consequence?

 

Yet to her—exiled from kin, adrift in a land that met her every gesture with suspicion—I remained a lone flicker of fellowship. In the crevices between her duties, she would recount, with the fervour of a bard preserving a dying oral tradition, the sagas of her village: the monsoon floods that swallowed huts whole, the jackal that stole her youngest brother’s sandal, the way the neem trees shivered at dusk as though haunted by the ghosts of starved ancestors. These tales, stitched together from memory and longing, she entrusted to me as one might entrust heirlooms to a fellow exile, and I received them with the solemnity they deserved, sensing even then that they were not mere stories but lifelines tethering her to a selfhood back home that a system of unequal wealth that brought her here had sought to erase.

 

Time eventually worked its corrosive magic. When we relocated to a villa enmeshed in the grasping orbit of my extended family, the censure that had once been sporadic hardened into doctrine. My cousins, now of older age and marinated in the brine of the typical youth conservatism, became both wardens and mirrors reflecting back a destiny I was expected to embrace. Slowly, insidiously, the empathy I had built within the maids’ room began to crumble; each reprimand from my mother (“Must you gawk like a beggar when she speaks?”), each snicker from an uncle (“Careful, or you’ll end up as dark as her!”), chiseled fissures in its foundation. By sixteen or so, the transformation was complete: I addressed Suwaida with the clipped courtesy of a stranger, my voice stripped of its former warmth, my eyes averted in indifference. The woman who had once cradled me through fevers, who had smuggled jalebi into my pockets when I wept over math lessons, was now 'the maid'—a functionary.

 

Years have unspooled since those days; I have long since fled Qatar’s gilded claustrophobia, and Suwaida, like so many casualties of childhood, has receded into the realm of the irretrievable. Yet in quieter hours, when the past looms phantasmal, I find myself bargaining with ghosts: What if we had never moved? What if my familys' hearts had been less calcified by caste? What if I had possessed the courage to defy, rather than capitulate? Such questions are the tax one pays for abandonment. Still, I clutch to a half-superstitious conviction—a child’s faith, perhaps—that our paths will converge again. When they do, I imagine her not as the aged servant of popular caricature as she is now, limping down the stairs carrying household items over her head, but as she exists in my memory: cross-legged on the floor, her sari a splash of cobalt against the drab walls, her voice weaving tales of flooded villages and neem trees as I, once more the turtle-boy with downturned eyes, lean in to listen. We will laugh, I think, not at the past’s absurdity but at the stubbornness of hope, that fragile, foolish thing that persists, like a weed in cracked concrete, long after the world has declared it extinct.
