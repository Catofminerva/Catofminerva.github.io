Correspondence Theory and Lexical Dependence

1. The Problem

Suppose we begin with a correspondence theory of truth: a statement \(S\) is true if and only if \(S\) corresponds to a fact \(F\) in the world. Now add the observation that the meaning of \(S\)—its particular concepts, terms, and structure—is fixed by some lexicon \(L\). If truth is nothing more than “matching” the world, but all matches are formulated within a specific lexicon, then the question arises: How exactly does the world provide a fact that the language can match?

2. Lexical Dependence and Its Implications

  1. Any given statement \(S\) has content only within a lexicon \(L\). We cannot make sense of (or even parse) “Snow is white” until we understand what “snow” and “white” mean in our shared language.
  2. To say that \(S\) “matches” or “corresponds” to a fact \(F\) presupposes that \(F\) is the relevant “portion” or “structure” of reality singled out by the conceptual terms of \(S\). But identifying which structure of reality counts as \(F\) requires that we already be operating with the categories, distinctions, and referential practices embedded in \(L\).
  3. If the correspondence theorist insists that “the world itself” contains a fact precisely matching \(S\) (as formulated in \(L\)), she is thereby committed to the view that reality must come “pre-partitioned” according to that very lexicon. Put differently, for each conceptual distinction in \(L\), the world itself would need to have an ontological counterpart waiting to be matched.
  4. It is implausible that a mind- and language-independent reality carries in its “fabric” any particular human conceptual scheme—whether modern chemistry, Aristotelian metaphysics, or otherwise. The world exerts causal constraints (e.g., if you call water “fire” and drink it, you get wet, not burned), but it does not supply the conceptual labeling we use to describe and make sense of those constraints.

A correspondence theorist might reply:

“Yes, statements are lexicon-dependent, but facts themselves exist independently of any conceptual framework. We merely label pre-existing facts with our words.”

But this fails to explain how one identifies or even recognizes which fact in the world corresponds to a given statement if not by invoking the conceptual apparatus of that very statement. The prior question is how to distinguish one “chunk” of reality from another in the absence of the categories or concepts used in \(L\). There is no neutral vantage point outside all lexical frameworks from which to witness the “pure” matching of statement to fact.

Conclusions

The quest for a “perfect” universal vocabulary—one that captures reality “as it really is,” without lexical or conceptual mediation—is like to trying to kiss your own lips. You are never outside your linguistic framework to compare it isomorphically to a wordless, unstructured “world.” Thus, You cannot call something “true” or “false” until you have agreed on the rules of the game—the shared lexicon and conceptual scheme in which the relevant claims are made. “Truth-talk” is therefore best viewed as a social and conceptual ritual: its coherence depends on participants’ alignment in both terminology and inferential standards. When we say “water is H₂O,” we are employing a highly successful chemical vocabulary that partitions the world into molecular kinds. This vocabulary has proven extraordinarily predictive and useful, but it is historically contingent: the atomic-molecular framework emerged at a particular time in culture. It is not forced upon us by some metaphysical property of reality that uses the labels “H,” “O,” or “molecule.” Another conceptual scheme (e.g., ancient Greek or Aristotelian) would parse “water” differently. Though modern chemistry has strong causal interactions backing it up—predictive success, —that success alone does not imply that the world literally “speaks” H₂O. It merely shows that this particular vocabulary aligns well with our goal in making predictions.

Thus, the notion that there is one ultimate vocabulary capturing the world “as it is” faces the same logical stumbling block as the attempt to verify your own statements from outside all conceptual schemes. You simply cannot get outside the lexicon you are using to check whether “it really matches” a fact in a purely language-independent world.
