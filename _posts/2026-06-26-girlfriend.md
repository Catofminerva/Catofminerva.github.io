title: "A Girlfriend is an Approximate Optimal Solution within Acceptable Range"

There is a conspiracy by which the human animal is prevented from saying aloud what it knows, though it knows it very well because the survival of every society depends upon its members collectively honouring the conspiracy. It is that human animal pairs, just as non-human animal pairs, simply settle for each other. The former, being as they are, a species of conscious beings, go on to create all sorts of fairy tales around this consummation; they sublimate their consciousness to various institutions (religions, countries, families) and one of the oldest of which, and one of the more consequential for their survival, is that of human marriage.

Nothing is more irrational than human marriage. I have done the calculation and I suspect a great many people have in private as well, since it is much too shameful to be said aloud. It is thus: assigning weights I would deny having and asking whether the person in front of me satisfies standards I have not and will never write down but can simply ‘feel’. People often come up with a description of how two people end up together. *We just clicked, it was love at first sight, fate put us together*, an inexhaustible supply of consolatory nonsense. It is very seldom that anyone confesses to the conspiracy of how they found their lover, which is that they ran a search over a space they did not, because they cannot, enumerate, and simply stopped the search at a point nowhere near provably best, but that nevertheless the act of stopping was good, correct, even the best and most fortunate thing that was ever done in their lives.

What would finding the actual optimum human partner require? One would have to assess every living person of the relevant description, run the comparison across all of them, and return the maximum. This is impossible in any modality available to a creature whose life is ephemeral and whose candidates are billions. The economist Herbert Simon described, in the nineteen-fifties, the thing we do in place of the impossible thing; we *satisfice*. What does this mean? An ugly word such as it is, welding-together of *satisfy* and *suffice*, but it boils down to the idea that, instead of hunting for the option that maximises your objective, by which is meant the quantity you are trying to make as large as possible, you instead fix an aspiration level, a threshold of ‘good-enough’, and seize the option that satisfies it and you stop. Schopenhauer had said as much already that the will that drives us is never quieted by its object, so that desire attained is merely desire transferred, and the satisficing of a partner is one more case of an organism accepting a finite settlement, clever enough to know that the infinite one it inherently craves is a chimera. ‘Man does not know what he wants’, says de Maistre, ‘he wants what he does not want; he does not want what he wants; *he wants to want*’.

The pool of persons one meets are a small and heavily conditioned remainder determined by the accident of where one was born, the language one was raised in, the work one drifted into, the doorway one happens to pass through on a Tuesday, the friends whose friends in time become one’s own. By the time a person is a living candidate, the lot has already been cut down by circumstances that owe nothing to one's preferences. The image represented in romance novels is that of a single perfect figure waiting somewhere to be found. *Somewhere* indexes a region of the space that time has already sealed shut. This figure therefore cannot be found in any modality a mortal possesses. No one likes to believe this about their own partners; that there is no invisible hand arranging the match and that there is only a constrained accident which the two of them will spend the rest of their lives redescribing as fate.

Given all this, arranged marriage is a perfectly sound, perfectly rational, procedure, and indeed very likely the only candid one. It reduces the search space by family and by locale and by the long catalogue of practical compatibilities the parents are tracking whether or not they would phrase it so. It stops early enough and commits without reservation; it accepts at the outset the irrationality that human consummation in fact requires, which is to go in blind and forgo the endless deliberation over rival candidates that delivers a person, soon enough, into paralysis. To enter blind is, in this one respect, the rational course. Whether the union is arranged by the parents or undertaken in the name of free affection, the underlying practice is the same, which is to say, a local search against a serviceable threshold. We can then pat our backs and proclaim we have found an approximate optimal solution within acceptable range. The arranged version of human unions merely conducts it in the open, indeed doing so with more information to hand and a good deal less deception of the self. What repels us in it is I think the fact that it utters the perverse calculation we have all agreed to keep behind our teeth, and so suspends, for as long as it speaks, the conspiracy by which the rest of us live.

In the final analysis, human consummation rests upon an irrational premise and the choice it demands is one reason can never make in advance. Why should a person elect, freely and for the whole of a life, one companion, when his attention may at any later hour be drawn to another he finds more agreeable, and whom he might with equal freedom elect instead? What one wants from a partner does not hold constant across the duration of their lives; persons change and the years spent besides one another revises one’s preferences, one's sense of what matters, the very standard the search was meant to serve, until what one comes to prize in the other is frequently a thing one had not the knowledge to want on the day they met. One cannot, in other words, declare in advance what one is optimising for because the act of optimising creates the specification. There is then no maximum standing behind the search to be approached or fallen short of; the standard does not precede the choice if it in fact is instantiated by it. One first commits and the feeling is constituted afterward (whether it is a true or false feeling is orthogonal), this being the only order that does not require the solving of an insoluble problem before one is allowed to begin. Therefore, the organism does not choose its object and proceed to love it. It arrives to an object by circumstance and then creates out of proximity and repetition and the need not to have erred, the sensation it will afterward call ‘love’.

Notice, however, that none of this may be said to the person it concerns. Grant that the analysis is correct. Grant that love is, when reduced to its gross technicality, an acceptable approximation arrived at almost arbitrarily under the pressure of time. One may never say this to the person one has chosen. ‘You are an acceptable approximation’ is a true proposition and it is also among the cruellest things one person could say to another. It is cruel because it is true. A true description of the relation uttered inside the relation, destroys the relation. It pertains only to the class of statements which must go unspoken by the parties if they are to remain true of them, which is to say it must be kept as a conspiracy.
