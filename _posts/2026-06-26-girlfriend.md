title: "A Girlfriend is an Approximate Optimal Solution within Acceptable Range"

There is a conspiracy by which the human animal is prevented from saying aloud what it knows, though it knows it very well because the survival of human societies depends upon its members collectively honouring the conspiracy. It is that human animal pairs, just as non-human animal pairs, simply settle for each other. The former, being as they are, a species of conscious beings, go on to create all sorts of fairy tales around this consummation; they sublimate their consciousness to various institutions (religions, countries, families) and one of the oldest of which, and one of the more consequential for their survival, is that of human marriage.

Nothing is more irrational than human marriage. Why should a person elect, freely and for the whole of a life, one companion, when his attention may at any later hour be drawn to another he finds more agreeable, and whom he might with equal freedom elect instead? What one wants from a partner does not hold constant across the duration of their lives; persons change and the years spent besides one another revises one’s preferences, one's sense of what matters, the very standard the search was initiated to serve, until what one comes to prize in the other is frequently a thing one had not the knowledge to want on the day they met. One cannot, in other words, declare in advance what one is optimising for because in the act of optimising one goes on to posit the specification into existence. There is then no perfect choice standing behind the search that one can meet or fall short of given that the standard does not precede the choice if it in fact is instantiated by it. One first commits and the feeling is constituted in retrospect (whether it is a true or false feeling is orthogonal), this being the only order that does not require the solving of an insoluble problem before one is allowed to begin. Humans therefore do not choose their object of love and proceed to love it but rather they happen upon the object by circumstance and then create out of proximity and repetition and the need not to have erred the sensation they will afterward call ‘love’.

People often come up with a much different description of how they ended up together. *We just clicked, it was love at first sight, fate put us together*, an inexhaustible supply of consolatory nonsense. It is very seldom that anyone confesses to the conspiracy of how they found their lover, which is that they ran a search over a space they did not, because they cannot, enumerate, and simply stopped the search at a point nowhere near provably best, but that nevertheless the act of stopping was good, correct, even the best and most fortunate thing that was ever done in their lives. The pool of persons one actually ends up with is a small and heavily conditioned remainder determined by the accident of where one was born, the language one was raised in, the work one drifted into, the doorway one happens to pass through on a Tuesday, the friends whose friends in time become one’s own. By the time a person is a living candidate, the lot has already been cut down by circumstances that owe nothing to one's preferences. There is no perfect figure waiting somewhere to be found. No one likes to believe this about their own partners; that there is no invisible hand arranging the match and that there is only a constrained accident which the two of them will spend the rest of their lives redescribing as fate.

What human consummation therefore requires is blind commitment. For what would finding the actual optimum human partner actually require? One would have to assess every living person of the relevant description and run the comparison across all of them and return the maximum. This is simply impossible in any modality available to an organism whose life is ephemeral and whose candidates are billions. Instead of hunting for the option that maximises your objective, by which is meant the quantity you are trying to make as large as possible, you instead fix an aspiration level, a threshold of ‘good-enough’, and seize the option that satisfies it and you stop. You will invariably, however much you think otherwise, want another partner, perhaps even for the sole sake of wanting. Schopenhauer described a blind, insatiable life-force that is the Will that drives us, never quieted by its object, so that desire attained is merely desire transferred. ‘Man does not know what he wants’, also says de Maistre, ‘he wants what he does not want; he does not want what he wants; *he wants to want*’. Today dating apps present you with an infinite number of possible partners, the next swipe may as well present the perfect one and the search simply never ends. 

Given all this, arranged marriage is a perfectly sound, perfectly rational, procedure, and indeed very likely the only candid one. It stops early enough and commits without reservation; it accepts at the outset the irrationality that human consummation in fact requires, which is to go in blind and forgo the endless deliberation over rival candidates that delivers a person, soon enough, into paralysis. To enter blind is, in this one respect, the rational course. Whether the union is arranged by the parents or undertaken in the name of free affection, the underlying practice is the same, which is to say, a local search against a serviceable threshold. We can then pat our backs and proclaim 'we have found an approximate optimal solution within acceptable range!'. The arranged version of human unions merely conducts it in the open, indeed doing so with more information to hand and a good deal less deception of the self. What repels us in it is I think the fact that it utters the perverse calculation we have all agreed to keep behind our teeth, and so suspends, for as long as it speaks, the conspiracy by which the rest of us in so-called 'free-societies' live.
