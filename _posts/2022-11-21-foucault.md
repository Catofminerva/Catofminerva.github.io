# Why Couldn't Anyone Be an Atheist in 1500?
*Epistemic status: a position I hold confidently in its weak form, less confidently in its strong form, and with a great deal of remaining uncertainty about how the strong and weak forms come apart. The piece is partly an attempt to figure out where the line is.*
[![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/b2533790-e75a-4554-97e6-008539516872_640x358.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb2533790-e75a-4554-97e6-008539516872_640x358.jpeg)

## I. The Febvre Puzzle
There is a question raised by Lucien Febvre, in a book published in 1942 under conditions that one suspects sharpened his interest in it, which I have been thinking about. The question is whether it was possible, in sixteenth-century France, to be an atheist.

The question sounds odd. Of course it was possible. People believe and disbelieve things in any century, and the sixteenth century was full of heretics, Protestants, occultists, libertines, and a great many people who said things in private that would have got them in serious trouble if said in public. Febvre was a careful historian and he knew all this. He spent four hundred pages working through the available evidence, which is considerable. He examined the polemical literature, the trial records, the libertine poets, the philosophical underground that flourished around the universities, the Italian materialists who had circulated their texts into France, and the various people who had been accused of atheism by their enemies. He was looking for one well-attested case of an actual sixteenth-century atheist, in something like the modern sense of the word, and his conclusion at the end of four hundred pages is that he could not find one. There were freethinkers. There were anti-clericals. There were heretics of every variety. There were people who said God did not care about specific things, or that the priests were liars, or that the resurrection was an allegory. There were not, anywhere in the available evidence, any people who had successfully disbelieved in God in the sense in which a contemporary atheist disbelieves in God.

This is, I want to insist, a strange finding. It is not a finding about the sociology of religion or the persistence of belief under pressure. It is a finding about something more basic, which is the *availability* of a particular position as a position. Febvre's claim is not that no one in 1500 dared to be an atheist. His claim is that the cognitive equipment necessary to hold the position (to hold it stably, with conviction, against the press of one's daily life) was not present in the linguistic and intellectual environment of the time. The vocabulary did not exist. The arguments did not exist. The sceptical frame of reference did not exist; there were sceptics, but their scepticism was always about specific theological claims rather than about the framework within which those claims were made. The word *atheist* was in circulation, but it did not mean what we mean by it; it was a term of abuse, applied to people who held the wrong religious views, not a self-description anyone would take up. To be an atheist in 1500, you would have had to assemble the position from scratch, against a culture in which the equipment for assembling it was not lying around, and against an inner life that had been shaped, from infancy, by the bells and the calendars and the rituals and the pervasive theological vocabulary in which all reflection was conducted. Nobody managed it. So far as Febvre could tell, nobody seriously tried.

Marc Bloch, in *Feudal Society*, makes a similar point about the medieval period more broadly. The whole picture of the world that medieval Europeans inhabited was theological in a way that is hard for us to reconstruct. It was not that they had a theology *and* a worldview, with the theology overlaid on top; the theology *was* the worldview, the substrate within which all thinking happened. The Bloch line that has stayed with me is that even the doubts that did surface “lacked any rational basis”, meaning not that the doubts were stupid, but that there was no scaffolding of independent inquiry to which the doubts could attach themselves and become serious. The doubts were like sparks struck against air; they did not catch on anything.
I want to start here because I think the Febvre puzzle is a hard puzzle and I think the standard ways of dissolving it do not work.
##II. Three Easy Dissolutions That Don't Work
Here are three responses one might give to Febvre's claim, in roughly the order I have heard them.
Response 1: People were too scared. On this view, there were plenty of atheists in 1500, but the Inquisition was real and the punishments for heresy were severe, so atheists kept their views to themselves. Febvre is just looking at the public record and missing the private reality.

This response does not survive contact with the evidence. The libertine and freethinking literature of the sixteenth century is large and quite candid. People said many shocking things and got away with most of them, especially in private circles and especially in writing intended for private circulation. The historical record contains an enormous quantity of *almost*-atheism - e.g., anti-clericalism, materialism, scepticism about miracles, arguments that God might be conceived in radically non-Christian ways, but it does not contain actual atheism. The claim is not that no one wrote down their atheist views; it is that no one *had* atheist views in the relevant sense. The almost-atheist material is exactly the kind of thing we would expect to find if Febvre is right and exactly the kind of thing we would not expect to find if Response 1 is right. (If everyone were a closet atheist, you would expect more aggressive almost-atheism in the underground literature, with the few public expressions being the tip of a hidden iceberg. Instead the underground literature is itself almost-atheist rather than atheist, which suggests the iceberg's tip is the iceberg.)

**Response 2: The category is anachronistic.** On this view, modern atheism is a particular cultural product of the post-Enlightenment world, and asking whether sixteenth-century people were atheists is like asking whether they were existentialists. The question is not interesting because the category does not apply.

This response is closer to right than Response 1, but it concedes the point Febvre is making rather than dissolving it. If modern atheism is a particular cultural product that did not exist in 1500, then we have given up the picture in which there is a stable, transhistorical question (does God exist?) to which different ages have given different answers, with some answers being correct and others incorrect. We have replaced that picture with one in which the question itself, in the form in which we ask it, is a product of historical conditions that did not obtain in 1500, and therefore the *correctness* of any particular answer is also indexed to those conditions. This is roughly the position I want to defend. But it is not a dissolution of Febvre's puzzle; it is a partial concession to it. Response 2, taken seriously, leads to anti-foundationalism.

**Response 3: The question is about psychology, not philosophy.** On this view, sixteenth-century people had the same cognitive capacities as we do, they just lived in a different cultural environment that made certain positions hard to articulate. Atheism was psychologically available; it just was not socially available.

This response is the most sophisticated of the three, and it is the one I want to spend the most time on, because I think it almost works and the way it almost-works is informative about what we actually mean by *availability*.

The intuition behind Response 3 is correct, in the limited sense that nobody is denying that sixteenth-century brains and modern brains are made of roughly the same neural equipment. The question is not biological. The question is whether *psychological availability*, abstracted from the social and linguistic environment in which a person actually thinks, is a coherent concept. I am not sure it is. To hold a position in any robust sense requires more than the bare neural capacity to entertain the relevant proposition. It requires the capacity to formulate the position in some vocabulary, to defend it against counterarguments one has actually encountered, to relate it to other things one believes, to resist the press of ordinary life in a way that requires constant ongoing maintenance, and to have access to enough other people who are doing similar work that one's position does not, over the long course of an actual life, simply dissolve under the weight of being unsharable. The sixteenth-century atheist would have had to do all of this with no models, no shared vocabulary, no community of fellow practitioners, no philosophical tradition to draw on, and against a cultural environment in which every bell and every calendar and every casual reference at the dinner table reaffirmed the framework he was supposed to be rejecting.

I think it is at least an open question whether anyone, of any era, can hold a complex philosophical position under those conditions for the full length of an adult lifetime. I am not sure I could. (I notice that I have many positions I currently hold which I am fairly sure I would not have held had I been born in different conditions. Some of these are positions I think are correct. The ones I think are correct, I think are correct *in part because* of the conditions that allow them to be held.) The Response 3 picture, in which positions float free of the conditions that make them holdable, is a picture I want to reject, not because I am denying that people have minds, but because I think the picture of mind it presupposes is wrong.

## III. The Anti-Foundationalist Position
Here is the position I want to defend, stated as briefly as I can, with the warning that the brief statement is going to overstate the strong form and the rest of the piece is partly about pulling it back.

There is no view from nowhere. The sentences that count as true, in any given linguistic community, count as true relative to a set of practices, vocabularies, criteria of evidence, and institutional structures that have themselves been produced by a particular history. The history could have gone differently and the sentences would then have been different. There is no further fact, beyond the practices and the history, that determines which set of sentences correctly *corresponds* to the world; the world causally pushes against our sentences (we cannot say *just anything*), but the world does not dictate which sentences to use, and the sentences we end up using are tools we have developed for the work we are trying to do, not transcriptions of an antecedent reality. ‘Truth’ is a property of sentences, sentences are tools, and the choice of tools is a question for us.

That is the strong form. The weak form is: even if there are some sentences that do correspond to the world in a robust metaphysical sense, the *interesting* questions about how communities come to count certain sentences as true, and to organise their lives around those countings, are answerable without invoking the metaphysical correspondence. The weak form is compatible with a lot of things the strong form is not. I will end up defending something in between.

## IV. What Foucault Adds to This

[![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/5c2fcafc-be2d-41f7-aa7f-0a01ad52eff5_750x726.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5c2fcafc-be2d-41f7-aa7f-0a01ad52eff5_750x726.jpeg)

[![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/563df9fc-0da5-4c29-9c63-034f70a9f44e_571x900.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F563df9fc-0da5-4c29-9c63-034f70a9f44e_571x900.jpeg)
The position I have just stated is more or less Richard Rorty's, and Rorty got most of it from Wittgenstein, Sellars, Davidson, and Quine, with some Dewey at the bottom. None of this is Foucault. Why does Foucault matter?

Foucault matters because the philosophical apparatus I have just described is, on its own, somewhat thin in its empirical content. Rorty tells us that vocabularies are tools and that we choose between them on pragmatic grounds, but he does not, in his own work, do very much of the actual work of showing how this happens in particular cases. Foucault did. Foucault spent his career producing extremely detailed historical case studies of how particular vocabularies came into being, what work they did once they existed, and what happened to the people whose lives were reorganised by their existence. Whatever one thinks of the specific Foucault interpretations (and many of them have been disputed by careful historians on points of fact), the *kind* of work Foucault did is the kind of work the Rortyan position needs in order to be more than a slogan. Foucault is the empirical lieutenant of the philosophical claim.

*History of Madness* is Foucault's first major book and, in my view, still his strongest. The basic claim is that ‘madness’, as a unified category of mental disorder requiring medical treatment, did not exist in any robust form before the late eighteenth century. There were people with what we would now call schizophrenia, depression, mania; there were people considered possessed by demons; there were people kept by their families as harmless eccentrics; there were people who lived as fools or holy fools or village idiots. What there was not, until quite recently, was a single category that grouped all of these as instances of a unified phenomenon called *mental illness*, distinct from physical illness, requiring the specific intervention of a specific class of professional. That category was *produced* in a particular historical moment, by particular institutional pressures (including the rise of the asylum, the professionalisation of medicine, the bureaucratic needs of the modern state), and once produced it began to do work that altered the lives of the people it now classified.

The standard objection to this kind of analysis is that it sounds like a denial that mental illness is real. Foucault sometimes encouraged this reading and his anti-psychiatry admirers ran with it, but I think the strong version of the reading is wrong, and I want to be clear about what the analysis is and is not committed to.

It is *not* committed to the claim that the brains of people we now diagnose with schizophrenia are not doing something distinctive. They are. The neurological and biochemical correlates of schizophrenia are well-documented, the heritability is substantial, the cross-cultural prevalence is more uniform than for most psychiatric conditions, and the underlying disturbance is quite real. The analysis is not denying any of this.

What the analysis *is* committed to is the claim that the *category* of schizophrenia - the way the disturbance is grouped, named, classified, treated, distinguished from other things, integrated into legal and welfare and family structures, and experienced from the inside by the person to whom it applies - is a historical product. The category could have been carved differently. It has, in fact, been carved differently in different periods, and the difference shows up in the diagnosis rates, in the treatments offered, in the patients' own experiences of their conditions, and in the social fates that follow from being categorised one way rather than another. Ian Hacking's example, which I think is the neatest one available, is the way the diagnostic criteria for schizophrenia in mid-twentieth-century European psychiatry shifted as Kurt Schneider's *First Rank Symptoms* became the standard list. Once auditory hallucinations were near the top of the list, more people were diagnosed, the people diagnosed were a different population, the patients themselves began to take their hallucinations more seriously, the public response shifted, and the trajectory of the diagnosis itself was altered. None of this is a denial that there is a real underlying disturbance. It is a description of how a real underlying disturbance gets *taken up* into a system of categories that does work in the world.

This is the point I want to draw out, because I think it is the most important payoff of the whole apparatus. Categories are not passive descriptions of an antecedent reality. They are interventions. They alter the things they classify, sometimes by altering how those things are treated and sometimes by altering how the classified things conceive of themselves. Hacking calls this the ‘looping effect'. Once you have a classification (*autistic, schizophrenic, fat, gay, introvert, gifted, traumatised*), the people who fall under it begin to live, partly, in the conceptual space the classification opens up. They make sense of their behaviour in its terms. They organise their lives around it. They join communities of others similarly classified. They take on therapies, prognoses, identities. And the classification, in turn, shifts as a result of how the classified people behave, because the diagnostic criteria are calibrated against the population, and the population has been altered by the diagnosis.

This is why I think the pedantic philosophical question - *but is schizophrenia REAL?* - misses the more interesting question, which is: what did the production of ‘schizophrenia' as a category do, and to whom, and is the category, on balance, doing work we should want it to be doing? The interesting question is normative and historical. The metaphysical question is, mostly, a distraction.

## V. The Christendom Case, Revisited
Now we can go back to the Febvre puzzle and say what is going on there.

Christendom, in Febvre's analysis, was not a body of doctrine that medieval Europeans happened to accept and that they could in principle have rejected. It was the substrate within which the capacity to accept or reject doctrines had been formed. The bells, the calendar, the guilds, the schools, the family rituals, the legal vocabulary, the categories of the self (sinner, penitent, saved, damned), the cosmological scheme, the political theory, the medical theory, the moral psychology, the theory of language, all of this was theological in such a thorough way that there was no neutral place from which one could survey the theology and decide whether to accept it. There was no *outside*. The mental tools with which one would have evaluated the theology were themselves products of the theology.

This is not an account of indoctrination or repression, although both of those existed. It is an account of how the conditions of intelligibility for a position get set. To be an atheist in any robust sense, you need a vocabulary in which God is one entity among others rather than the substrate of intelligibility itself; you need a public conception of *evidence* that is independent of testimony from religious authorities; you need a community of others holding similar views or at least a tradition of texts you can read in private; you need the conceptual distinction between *reasons* and *causes* to be established and stable; you need at least a few of the key sceptical arguments to have been formulated by someone, somewhere, in a form you can engage with. None of this was lying around in 1500. The distinctions had not yet been made. The vocabulary had not yet been developed. The community did not exist. The texts had not been written. (Most of the texts that *had* been written by sixteenth-century atheists, such as they were, would have been illegible to other sixteenth-century atheists, because the vocabulary was being invented in real time and was idiosyncratic to each writer.)

The Febvre puzzle, on this analysis, is not a puzzle. It is a particularly clear illustration of a more general phenomenon, which is that positions are holdable relative to the conditions of holdability that obtain in a given time and place. We do not, as historical agents, have unlimited access to all possible positions. We have access to the positions our circumstances have made available, and the positions our circumstances have made available are a function of the entire prior history of how those circumstances came to be the way they are.

This generalises. The thing it generalises to is the claim that *we are not exempt*. The same kind of analysis that we apply to sixteenth-century Christendom can in principle be applied to twenty-first-century secular liberalism, and when applied it reveals a roughly similar situation: there are positions the contemporary mind cannot easily hold, because the conditions of holdability are not present. Various forms of pre-modern religiosity are in this category for most contemporary Westerners. So are various forms of communalism that take the family or the tribe rather than the individual as the basic unit of moral concern. So, increasingly, are the strict materialism of certain mid-twentieth-century left-wing politics, and the Christian humanism of the early Cold War, and a number of other positions that were once the default for intelligent people and are now nearly impossible to occupy with full conviction. None of this means those positions are wrong. It means that the conditions of their holdability are not present in our environment, and one would have to do an enormous amount of reconstructive work to hold them, of the kind that the sixteenth-century atheist would have had to do in reverse.

## VI. Why This Doesn't Collapse Into Relativism
The standard objection at this point is that the position I have just laid out collapses into relativism. If positions are holdable only relative to the conditions of holdability that obtain in a given time and place, then what is the difference between a position being *true* and a position being *currently holdable*? Has not the whole apparatus of truth been replaced by the apparatus of cultural fashion?

The short answer is no, but the short answer requires some unpacking.

The position is not that anything goes. The position is that the world *causally* pushes against our sentences, even though it does not *semantically* dictate them. There are sentences we cannot say with a straight face because the world makes them too hard to maintain. ‘The cat is on the mat’ survives because, if there is a cat and there is a mat and the cat is on the mat, the sentence is supported by the kind of evidence ordinary cat-mat speech-communities have organised themselves around. ‘The Earth is six thousand years old’ does not survive, in any community that has organised itself around the kinds of evidence geology and astronomy provide, because that evidence pushes back against the sentence too hard for it to remain a load-bearing claim within the community's web of belief. This is not a denial of the world's role; it is a specification of the world's role. The world is the thing that prevents us from being able to say just anything. What it does not do is select among the sentences that survive its causal pressure, because there are many such sentences and the choice between them is up to us.

For questions that are heavily constrained by the world's causal pushing (most of empirical science, most of ordinary perceptual judgement, most of well-formed mathematics) the question of which sentences to use is overdetermined by the constraints, and there is something close to convergence over time across many different starting points. For questions that are weakly constrained by the world's causal pushing (most of normative ethics, most of social ontology, most of the questions about how to organise human life) the question of which sentences to use is genuinely up to us, and the differences between traditions and historical periods are correspondingly larger. The anti-foundationalist position is not committed to denying that the first kind of question exists. It is committed to denying that the interesting questions are mostly of the first kind. They are mostly of the second kind, and treating them as if they were of the first kind is the foundationalist mistake.

The position I hold is that some questions are heavily underdetermined and some are not, that the heavily underdetermined ones include most of the ones we actually fight about, and that pretending the underdetermination does not exist by dressing our preferences up as discoveries is the rhetorical move I am trying to refuse.

## VII. More Objections
I want to spend a few paragraphs on the strongest objections to the position I have just sketched.
**Objection: anti-foundationalist position is self-defeating.** If all positions are products of the conditions of holdability that obtain in a given time and place, then so is the anti-foundationalist position. So why should anyone hold it?

This is the objection I take most seriously and the one I am least confident I have a fully satisfying answer to. The honest version of the answer is that yes, the anti-foundationalist position is itself a product of conditions, and one should be appropriately humble about it. We are not at the end of history. There may be ways of thinking, currently inaccessible to us, from the vantage of which the anti-foundationalist position will look like a mistake of our particular cultural moment, the way nineteenth-century positivism now looks like a mistake of its moment. I cannot rule this out and I do not see how I could.

What I can say is that the position is *available* to me now, in a way that the alternative is increasingly not, and that the work of holding it is the work of sustaining a particular vocabulary against the press of competing vocabularies in the present cultural moment. I hold it because it seems to me to make better sense of more of the things I have to make sense of than the alternatives do, and because the costs it imposes on me seem lower than the costs the alternatives impose. This is not a foundationalist defence of anti-foundationalism. It is an anti-foundationalist defence of anti-foundationalism, which is the only kind of defence the position permits and which has the appropriate kind of humility built into it.

I notice that this answer will not satisfy a hard-line foundationalist, and I am not sure it should. The argument between foundationalism and anti-foundationalism has, to a non-trivial extent, the structure of an argument over which language one prefers to conduct the argument in. There is no neutral language in which to settle the meta-question. This is awkward but I do not think it is a defect of the position; I think it is a feature of the situation we are actually in.

## VIII. What This Is Good For
I have been going on for some thousands of words now and I want to end with something concrete. What does the anti-foundationalist position let one *do*, that the alternative does not?

Three things, in increasing order of importance.

It lets one historicise. The Foucauldian work of figuring out where particular categories came from, what work they did when they arrived, who they helped and who they hurt, and what the alternatives were - this work is much harder to do if one is committed to the categories being descriptions of a fixed reality. The anti-foundationalist position licenses a particular kind of investigation that the foundationalist position does not, and the investigation tends to be illuminating about a wide range of things (mental illness, sexual identity, criminality, race, nationality, the family, the modern state) that are otherwise hard to think about clearly.

It lets one disagree without metaphysics. A great many disputes that present themselves as disputes about reality turn out to be disputes about how to use a word. The dispute about whether *trans women are women* is one example I have elaborated on elsewhere; the dispute about whether *international law is really law* is another one I have elaborated on elsewhere; the dispute about whether Pluto is a planet is a third. The foundationalist framing produces a particular kind of doomed discussion in which each side accuses the other of misdescribing the reality, while the actual disagreement is about which use of the term would best serve which purposes. The anti-foundationalist position lets one see this clearly and have the actual argument rather than the proxy argument. This is, I think, a substantial pragmatic gain.

It lets one accept that we might be wrong. The most underrated benefit of the anti-foundationalist position, I think, is that it makes one's own current views feel appropriately provisional. If one's vocabulary is a tool that has been developed under particular conditions for particular purposes, then one's vocabulary is also revisable, and the project of revising it is part of the ongoing work of being a person who thinks. This is the kind of intellectual humility that lets one hold one's positions firmly while remaining genuinely open to the possibility that they are mistaken in ways one currently cannot see. I find this freeing, in a way that I think the foundationalist temperament does not.

## IX. Coda
Going back, finally, to the Febvre puzzle. The reason no one in 1500 could be an atheist is, on the analysis I have just offered, the same reason no one in 2025 can hold any number of positions that may, in 2500, look like obvious truths that everyone of the period was somehow blind to. We are all, in this sense, the medievals of someone's future. The atheists of 1500 are not strange because their cultural moment failed to deliver up to them a position that was floating around in the metaphysical ether waiting to be grasped. They are unstrange because their cultural moment had not yet produced the conditions under which that particular position could be assembled and held. Our cultural moment has its own such gaps, and we are no more able to inspect them than they were able to inspect theirs.

The philosophical position I have been defending is that this is the situation, that it has always been the situation, and that the work of being thoughtful in it is the work of making one's vocabulary as fit-for-purpose as possible in the conditions one happens to find oneself in, while remaining open to the possibility that one's purposes themselves are products of the conditions and may need to change as the conditions do. I do not know how to live in any other way. I am not sure anyone ever has. I think the people in 1500 who came as close to atheism as the conditions of their moment permitted were doing essentially what I am doing now, with different materials, and I think their efforts deserve our recognition rather than our condescension. They were trying to think, against the press of an environment that made the thinking hard, with the tools that environment had given them. We are still doing this. We are just doing it with different tools.
