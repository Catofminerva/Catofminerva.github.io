A confession to begin with. I had been waiting for the opening ceremony of the Qatar World Cup for the better part of a decade, and when it finally arrived the BBC chose to spend cut it off for  a panel discussion about my country's human rights record. Migrant workers, the kafala system, the criminalisation of homosexuality, the stadiums and the men who had died building them, the whole familiar reel of statistics over grayscale B-roll, narrated in that particular register british broadcasters reach for when they want you to know a tragedy is taking place somewhere inconvenient.

a clarification, before this gets away from me. i am, without hedging, pro-human-rights, though i'll hedge that the phrase wants cashing out properly, since i am an academic and we cannot help ourselves. The kafala system extracts too much from people who have too little. The criminal law as it stands is indefensible. I had been writing and arguing about both for years before any English broadcaster discovered them, and I would like to think I will still be doing so when the next World Cup has rendered the topic uninteresting again. The substance was not what bothered me.

What bothered me was harder to put my fingers on, and took the rest of the tournament to work out. I want to try here.

The first thing I noticed was that the panel was not, in any real sense, addressed to me, or to anyone in Doha. It was addressed to a British audience that already agreed with its conclusions, and its rhetorical shape was that of a confirmation. None of the participants seemed to imagine that a Qatari might be watching, or that if one were, he might have something to add. The format did not have a slot for that. There was the anchor, and there were the guests, and there was the absent country, named often and asked nothing.

This is a familiar enough complaint that I want to be careful with it, because I do not want to slide into the easier and less honest version, which is that Western criticism of non-Western states is therefore hypocritical or imperial or in bad faith and can be dismissed. I do not believe that. The criticisms were largely correct. The men building the stadiums had largely been treated as the panel said they had been treated. A bad argument made on television does not falsify the claim it is defending, and the discomfort of being lectured is not, by itself, an objection to the lecture.

What I think I was reacting to was something narrower, and more interesting to me as a problem. It was the discovery, in real time, that the vocabulary I had been using my whole adult life like rights, dignity, consent, abuse, does different work depending on who is speaking it and to whom. In a seminar room in London, those words had felt to me like tools of expression. On the BBC that night they sounded different. The sentences were the same sentences. The grammar of moral seriousness was identical. And yet what was happening when a British broadcaster used them in front of a British audience about my country, during the opening ceremony, was plainly not what happens when I use them with a colleague over coffee, or with my uncle over dinner, or in a paper. Something had been added to them by the staging. Or something had been taken away.

It took me a while to see what. I think it is this: in the seminar room and at the dinner table, the words are *load-bearing*. If I tell my uncle that the kafala system is unjust, he can disagree, and then I have to actually produce the argument, in front of him, in a voice he will recognise as belonging to someone he knows. The claim does work because it might fail. On television, in front of a domestic audience that had already arrived at the verdict before the show began, the words do not have to bear any weight. Nothing is being decided and no one is being persuaded. The function of language is no longer argumentative; it is liturgical. It marks the speakers and the audience as the kind of people who care about such things, in distinction from the kind of people, off-camera and elsewhere, who do not. An in-group vs an out-group. 

This, I think, is the thing that got under my skin, i.e., not the criticism but the liturgy and my own complicated position inside it. Because of course I am, in some respects, the audience for that liturgy. I was educated in Britain. I read the same papers. I know the gestures from the inside. I had not previously appreciated how much of what I took to be moral discourse was, when looked at from a slightly different angle, social discourse, a way of arranging *us* in relation to *them*, and how thoroughly the moral content can survive being put to that secondary use without anybody noticing the substitution.

By the quarter-finals nobody on British television was talking about migrant workers any more, and by the final the tournament had become, for its broadcasters, what it had always been for me, a football match, won by Argentina in a manner that justified the wait. Whatever had been at stake in those opening seminars, it apparently was not urgent enough to survive a good group stage. That tells you something about what the seminars were for, and it is not flattering, but I want to resist the temptation to make too much of it. The men in the labour camps are still in the labour camps. The law is still the law. The cameras leaving has not changed any of that, and neither did the cameras arriving. Which brings me back to the question of what the cameras were doing there in the first place, and whether anything they did mattered, and to whom.

### Was it a Clash of Civilisations?

What I have so far told only one half of a story, though, and the other half is the part that I find harder to write about, because I am implicated in it.

The campaign did not merely fail to persuade. It produced its inverse. Within weeks of the tournament's opening I was watching Qataris of my acquaintance, people who in any other month might have shrugged at the question, working themselves into a position considerably more reactionary than the one they had started from. The criticisms had landed, in a sense; they had simply landed the wrong way up. Suddenly there was a moral panic about an infiltration of sexual minorities into the country, as though none had ever lived here, as though they had been smuggled in among the German fans. Rainbow flags began to acquire a significance out of all proportion to anything they could plausibly do or mean. A pride flag confiscated by a steward at the Khalifa Stadium became, in the local imagination, a foiled invasion. Friends who I had previously heard speak with some sympathy about the men they knew or suspected, and who would not in private have considered it any of their business, were now posting clips of imams about civilisational decline.

The vocabulary of the response was, I noticed, almost entirely borrowed. *Orientalism, cultural imperialism, Western exceptionalism, hypocrisy*: every one of these is a word I have used, often in the seminar room, often correctly. They have real referents and they describe real phenomena. What was strange was watching them be wielded by people who had not, until that month, displayed any particular interest in the academic critique of empire, and who picked them up the way one picks up a stick during a fight. i.e., for what they could be used to hit.

This is, I want to insist, a separate phenomenon from the question of whether the words are *true*. It is perfectly possible for "this coverage is Orientalist" to be a correct description of a piece of broadcasting and also to be functioning, in the mouth of the person saying it, as a way of changing the subject from a kafala contract he would rather not discuss. Both things can be the case at once. In fact, I think both things were the case, at scale, throughout the tournament. The defensive vocabulary was not wrong and was merely doing a different job from the one its content advertised.

I should be honest about the fact that I caught myself doing it too. There were moments during the group stage when I would read a particular British column and feel my own arguments rearranging themselves in my head into a shape closer to my cousin's than I would have liked. The rearrangement was not at the level of belief (I did not start, mid-November, to think the kafala system was acceptable) but at the level of salience. Which thing did I want to talk about, and to whom, and now? Faced with a foreign audience already convinced of my country's barbarism, the part of me that wanted to discuss the labour reforms went quiet, and the part of me that wanted to point out that Britain had spent the last two centuries doing things considerably worse than confiscating a flag became conversational. I do not think I am unusual in this. I think it is, in fact, the ordinary mechanism by which a population is moved away from its own better instincts, and I think it operated on most of the people I know during those four weeks, in proportion to how much of the foreign coverage they happened to consume.

The standard name for what then unfolded is the clash of civilisations, and one of the small humiliations of the tournament was watching Huntington's old book exhumed by people who would not, in calmer weather, have given it the time of day. The clash was certainly available as a frame. There was the spectacle of Qatari clerics handing pamphlets to bewildered Argentinian fans on the corniche. There was a contingent of Muslims abroad, including, strangely, some Rohingya, raising photographs of Mohammed bin Salman and cheering the Saudi side past Argentina. There were the German players' covered mouths during the team photograph. There were the Palestinian armbands, which began the tournament as an expression of solidarity and ended it, in some hands, as a riposte to the rainbow. The footage, taken together, lent itself to the older narrative of two civilisations, one regression, one progress, locked into the loop in the way Huntington said they would be.

I think the Huntington frame is wrong, but I do not think it is wrong for the usual reasons people give. The usual move is to point out that "civilisations" are internally heterogeneous, which is true and trivial. The deeper problem is that the frame mistakes a symptom for a structure. What looked from the outside like two ancient blocs colliding was in fact something much more recent and much more contingent, which to say, two media ecologies, each with its own incentives, each producing content largely for domestic consumption, briefly pointed at one another by the accident of a sporting fixture. The Qatari clerics had been giving those pamphlets to one another for years; the cameras simply caught them doing it to a foreigner. The German team's gesture was, in the first instance, a German political event, addressed to German voters. What appeared on screen as civilisational confrontation was, in most cases, two parallel domestic performances that happened to be photographed in the same frame.

This matters because the casualties of the supposed clash were not the civilisations. The civilisations did fine. The casualties were the people in whose name the rhetoric was nominally being deployed, and who, in both directions, were the ones least visible on either set of screens. The migrant workers were not at the press conferences. The Qatari women I knew who had complicated views about the kafala system, and more complicated views about the broadcast lectures, were not on the panels. The men whose private lives were the supposed object of all this concern were, predictably, not standing up at half-time to clarify what they would and would not like done in their name. The argument had been arranged in such a way that it could only be conducted over them, and the louder it became the less audible they were within it. This is, I think, the point I most wanted to make about the tournament, and the point I find hardest to make stick, because it sounds, when written down, like the kind of balanced observation that flatters everyone and commits to nothing. I do not mean it that way. I mean that on the specific question of whether the lives of those people went better or worse for the four weeks of attention, I think the answer is worse, and that the worsening was a direct consequence of the form in which the attention was paid.

### Incitement to Discourse

I want now to make a more difficult argument, because the tournament gave me the occasion to think it through properly for the first time, and because I have not seen it made well in the places it most needs making.

The case against Qatar's treatment of sexual minorities, as it was presented to British and American audiences during the World Cup, was a case about *people*. There were gay Qataris; the state criminalised them; the state should stop. The structure of the argument was familiar from a thousand earlier campaigns - slavery, suffrage, Soviet Jewry - and its emotional oomph was the language of liberation. Somewhere behind a closed door there were persons whose 'true' nature had been forced into hiding, and the door needed opening, and the West, as it happened, had keys.

I do not think the argument was wrong in the way its opponents in Doha said it was wrong. They said it was hypocritical, and selectively applied, and culturally arrogant. All of this was true and none of it touched what I think is a more interesting problem, which was that the argument was making a philosophical assumption it had not noticed it was making, and the assumption was load-bearing for everything that followed. The assumption was that *gay people* are a kind of thing that exists prior to the discourse about them, that the closed door is a real door, with a real person behind it, who has a real identity, and that the work of liberation is to bring that identity into the open where it can take its place among the recognised forms of human life. This is, I want to say, not obviously false; in some societies, including the one the BBC was broadcasting from, it is something close to true. But it is not the kind of claim that travels well, and the tournament was, among other things, a four-week demonstration of what happens when one tries to make it travel without first asking whether it can.

Joseph Massad made the case at length twenty years ago in *Desiring Arabs*, and I do not need to rehearse the whole of it here. The short version, for the reader who has not read him, is this. Same-sex acts have a long history in the Arab world. Same-sex identities, in the modern Western sense - the sense in which a person is gay in roughly the way a person is left-handed, as a settled fact about the shape of an interior life, available for self-ascription and political organisation - do not. What exists, and has existed for a long time, is a great deal of what people actually do, conducted under a great variety of social arrangements, most of them tolerated in practice when they are not advertised, structured by who one is to whom rather than by what one is in oneself. The modern Western category, when introduced into this setting, does not name something that was already there waiting for a name, but it rather produces something. It calls a population into existence, and gives that population a single intelligible shape, and once the shape exists it can be defended, attacked, surveilled, legislated about, and, eventually, killed.

This is not a comfortable thing to say in either of the audiences that might be listening. To the Western reader it sounds like a denial of the reality of the people whose freedom is at stake. To the conservative Qatari reader it sounds like license. Neither hearing is what I mean.

I will start with the easier of the two. None of my school friends, when I was growing up, identified themselves as gay. Some of them did things, with one another and with others, that a Western observer would have unhesitatingly classified under that label. They did not so classify themselves, and *not* - I want to be very clear about this - because the cultural milieu had failed to provide them with the necessary vocabulary, leaving a real and felt identity inarticulate in their throats. They were not closeted. They were not in denial. They were not waiting for a more permissive society in which they could finally 'come out'. They were doing what they were doing, and they thought about it under descriptions that did not map cleanly onto the Western grid, and they got on with their lives. The picture in which everyone, everywhere, has always been gay-or-straight, with the only variable being whether the surrounding society lets them say so, is a picture that fits some societies and not others, and I have lived in one of the others, and I am telling you what it looked like from the inside.

The philosophical view that lets me say this without lapsing into the relativism that it is sometimes mistaken for is one I have argued for in more technical settings and will only sketch here. To *have* a concept, on the view I think correct, is to be a competent participant in a public language-game in which that concept does inferential work. Concepts are not little tokens stored privately in heads, awaiting either expression or repression; they are positions in a structure of moves people make in giving and asking for reasons. What it is for the concept 'gay' to be operative in a society is for there to be a stable set of inferences licensed by self-ascription of that term - inferences about whom one might marry, what one's parents will say, how one's employer will react, what one's political commitments are likely to be, which bars one drinks at, which novels one reads, which arguments one has on the internet. Where those inferences are in place, the concept has uptake. Where they are not, the concept is not yet a concept in that society, even if the word is intelligible and the practices it would in other societies pick out are present.

The Western activist who hears this tends to assume it must be a bad-faith move, a way of denying that there are gay Arabs in order to justify the state's treatment of them. It is not. The acts are real; the suffering is real; the criminal law is unjust. What is being denied is a particular *metaphysical* claim about the relation between the acts and an interior identity that supposedly underlies them. One can deny that claim and still hold, on entirely independent grounds, that the criminalisation of those acts is wrong. I do hold this. I have always held it. The argument I am making here is about which interventions, in which vocabulary, addressed to whom, are likely to make things better, and which are likely to make them worse, and the answer depends on getting the underlying anthropology approximately right.

To make the point concrete, here is something I have spent some time with and find more illuminating than any number of NGO reports: the *kasrat*, a folk genre popular among Saudi youth, in which a singer narrates, often with considerable directness, a love affair with another boy. One of the songs I have in mind opens with a school. A boy has caught his eye during recess, has cuffed his heart, has tortured him with a single look. He cannot stop thinking about him; he comes to school every day for the chance to pass his classroom in the corridor, the *asyāb*; he lies awake on burning coals through Wednesday and Thursday and Friday, waiting for Saturday, when he will see him again. The song is not coded. The song is not a cry for liberation from a society that will not let it speak. The song is, on the contrary, articulating an experience under descriptions that the surrounding culture recognises and to a large extent permits, in a register that does not require, and would in fact be ruined by, the imposition of a Western identity grid. There are many such songs. They are sung very casually.

I am not idealising the situation. The same culture that produces the kasrat also produces the religious police, and the same religious police that ignored a great deal in practice could, when sufficiently provoked, ruin a life. There was a famous exchange - preserved in a 1958 telegram from the American ambassador, Donald Heath, which I came across in the archives - in which a religious police officer arrived at the door of the Mufti, Ibn Ibrahim, to report a serious offence by the Mufti's son. The Mufti asked what the offence was. *Sodomy*, said the officer. *Oh*, said the Mufti, *I thought it was something serious, like smoking in the street*. The story circulates as a joke, and it is a joke, but it is also a fairly accurate description of a sensibility, one in which the public order had its priorities and the bedroom had its own, and the two were kept formally distinct in a way that the contemporary West, with its insistence that the personal is the political, finds difficult to recognise as anything other than hypocrisy.

The colonial encounter and the modernisations that followed it disturbed this arrangement, but they did not, until very recently, replace it. Nineteenth-century reformers, often in collaboration with European missionaries and lawyers, succeeded in importing a great deal of Victorian Christian moralism into Islamic jurisprudence, reading older texts through newer lenses and producing, in some areas  (contraception, abortion, women's testimony) interpretations considerably more restrictive than the medieval ones they claimed to be recovering. (This is not a niche point. The standard modern Arab consensus on contraception, for example, would have surprised the Hanafi jurists who wrote the relevant texts.) But the wholesale replacement of an act-centred sexual culture with an identity-centred one is a much later development, and where it has happened, it has happened in narrow strata: the Westernised elite, the English-speaking professional class, the milieu from which the BBC's Qatari interlocutors during the tournament were largely drawn. These are the people who have, to varying degrees, acquired the concept; they are not most of the country.

What happened during the tournament, in this analysis, was not the long-overdue arrival of a liberatory discourse in a benighted region. It was the abrupt nationalisation of an identity question that had not previously been a national question at all. Acts that had been managed, in the older arrangement, by a combination of discretion, tacit accommodation, and selective enforcement were dragged into the centre of public discourse, where they could no longer be managed in those ways. The result, predictably, was that they began to be managed in newer and worse ones. Once the question is *are you for or against gay rights*, and the question has been put in those terms by a foreign broadcaster, in the middle of a national event, in the explicit framing of our civilisation against yours, there is no available answer that leaves the previous arrangements intact. The discreet practices become legible as a national problem. The state, which had previously been content to look away in most cases, is now obliged to be seen looking. The ordinary Qatari, who had previously had no strong views on the question and considerable practical tolerance for what his neighbours did, finds himself enrolled, by way of the satellite news and the WhatsApp forward, in a civilisational defence he had not previously known he was conducting. And the men whose lives are the supposed object of the whole debate find that the closet they had not previously been in has been built around them, retroactively, by the  campaign that was supposed to be opening the door.

I want to make one more point before leaving this section, because without it the argument can be misread as a defence of the *status quo ante*, and it is not that.

The counter-discourse that arose in Doha during the tournament, and which presented itself as an authentic Islamic response to Western cultural imperialism, was nothing of the sort. Its idioms were almost entirely borrowed -and not from the Islamic tradition, which on these questions is older and stranger and more interesting than its current self-appointed defenders allow - but from the American Christian Right of the last forty years. The vocabulary of family decline, of grooming, of the social contagion of trans identification, of medicalised deviance, of the protection of children against an internationally co-ordinated threat, every one of these was imported, and most of them through the same media channels that brought the Western liberal critique. My cousins, who did not grow up in the country my parents grew up in, are now fluent in two competing American culture wars and not particularly fluent in any tradition older than either of them. They reach for Joe Rogan and Tucker Carlson the way, a generation earlier, their parents reached for Nasser and Faisal, and for many of the same reasons insofar these are the available idioms in which one can register a complaint about the present without having to do the harder work of articulating it from one's own materials.

This is the problem with supposed clash of civilisations framing. The two sides are not, in fact, civilisations colliding. They are two productions of the same global media economy, addressed to overlapping audiences, using premises about identity and visibility and the civilisational stakes of sex that are, at the level of philosophical structure, identical. They differ only in which side of the resulting binary they cheer for. The conservative Qatari who denounces the rainbow flag and the American liberal who waves it agree, without realising it, that gay is a basic human kind, that visibility is the natural medium of its political life, and that what is at stake in the question of how a state treats it is the moral standing of an entire civilisation. They are having the same argument. They are taking opposite sides in it. And the position I am trying to articulate (that the argument is itself an artefact of a particular historical moment, and that it has displaced older and in some respects more humane arrangements without thereby making the people it was nominally about any safer) is a position that has, by design, no place in either camp's self-presentation. To hold it is to be heard by both sides as a traitor to the other.

This is part of why writing it down is hard, and why I have put it off for the better part of three years, and why the tournament has been useful to me as an occasion, in that it gave me, finally, an example concrete enough that the argument could be made about something rather than in the air.
