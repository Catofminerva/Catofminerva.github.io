There is a particular kind of book that can only be written when an archive opens that the writer never expected to see, and Katherine Verdery's *My Life as a Spy* is one of them. Verdery is an American anthropologist who spent the better part of two decades, beginning in 1973, doing ethnographic fieldwork in Transylvanian villages. She was not, as she is at pains to point out and as the available evidence supports, a spy. She was a working academic with a Berkeley Ph.D., a series of grants, and the ordinary set of professional obligations that academic fieldwork involves. The Romanian Securitate, however, was of a different opinion, and after the fall of Ceaușescu in 1989 the new state began allowing those who had been surveilled to read what the secret police had written about them, and Verdery eventually applied, and the file she received ran to nearly three thousand pages. The book is her account of reading it.

I want to write about the book partly because it is good and partly because the question it ends up turning on is one I have been thinking about for some years now, which is the question of whether the kind of academic work I and people like me do can be sensibly described as innocent. The Securitate's basic accusation against Verdery, sustained across two decades and many hundreds of internal memos, was that she was not really an anthropologist; she was a CIA operative using anthropological work as cover. This is, on the face of it, false, and Verdery is quite clear that it is false, and I believe her. What is more interesting than the falseness of the accusation, though, is the fact that there is a sense in which the accusation was making contact with something real that the obvious denial does not quite dispose of. Verdery was not a spy. The discipline she was working in had been, in the relevant sense, a spy on more occasions than is comfortable, and was funded and structured in ways that made the Securitate's suspicion not paranoid in the strict sense but something closer to over-interpretation of a pattern that was, broadly, there. The interesting question the book raises, and which Verdery is too honest to dodge, is how an honest fieldworker should think about the fact that her honesty is not, by itself, sufficient to dissolve the structural complicity of the work she is doing.

A small detour through the history of the discipline, since most readers of anthropology in English will know some of this and most readers of everything else will not. Modern anthropology was, in its formative period, almost entirely a creature of empire. Malinowski's Trobriand fieldwork was conducted under wartime restrictions imposed by the Australian colonial administration that funded part of it. Evans-Pritchard's Sudanese work was published with the explicit acknowledgement that its findings would be of use to the colonial government, and his later work in the Sudan Political Service made the dual-use status of the research more or less explicit. American anthropology has its own version of the story in which Franz Boas, in 1919, published a letter in *The Nation* accusing four unnamed colleagues of having used anthropological cover for intelligence work in Central America during the First World War, and the discipline censured *him*, not them, for the impertinence of saying so in public. His student Ruth Benedict, twenty-five years later, was producing studies of Japanese national character for the U.S. Office of War Information, work she did not regard as compromised because she did not regard the war as a morally ambiguous one. RAND consulted anthropologists routinely during the Cold War. The Human Terrain System embedded anthropologists with American counterinsurgency units in Iraq and Afghanistan in the 2000s, and the resulting professional crisis in the American Anthropological Association produced a code of ethics that papered over the question rather than settling it. None of this is hidden. It is not even particularly contested. It is the recognised institutional history of the discipline, available in any of several careful histories, of which David Price's books are the most thorough.


The Securitate, in other words, was wrong about Verdery in the biographical sense and right about anthropology in the structural sense. They had a model of the discipline in which an American with a notebook arriving in a Romanian village to ask questions about kinship and cooperative organisation was, on the available evidence, more likely than not to be working for someone with broader interests than the kinship and the cooperatives. This was a bad description of Verdery but a defensible description of the average case across the relevant population. Her file is, among other things, the record of an institution applying a base rate to an individual and getting the individual wrong while getting the population approximately right. It is a particular kind of injustice and one that anyone who works in a profession with a compromised history will recognise from the inside.

This is the part of the book where I have to insert a paragraph about my own experience and where, in the interests of honesty, I am going to leave the gap empty and trust the reader to understand why. The point I would make, if I were going to make it, is that anyone who does academic work in a part of the world with an active intelligence apparatus has had at least a few moments of wondering what the conversation they are currently having looks like in some other room, and that the wondering does not require any actual surveillance to take place in order to do its corrosive work on the fieldworker's sense of being a single person doing a single thing.

Which brings me to the Pirandello. Pirandello's *One, No One, and One Hundred Thousand* is a strange, short, somewhat exasperating novel from 1926 in which a man called Vitangelo Moscarda discovers, when his wife mentions it casually, that his nose tilts slightly to the right, and that this fact about his face, which he has lived inside for years without noticing, is something everyone else has been noticing without telling him. From this small revelation Moscarda develops, over a few hundred pages, the conviction that the self he takes himself to be is not the self any of the people around him are actually responding to. They have each constructed a Moscarda of their own, on the basis of partial evidence and their own preoccupations, and the various Moscardas they are dealing with are not the same person and are not, in any of their cases, the person Moscarda thinks he is. There is no central Moscarda from whose vantage point the others can be assessed and dismissed as misperceptions. There are only the many Moscardas the world has produced, on top of the additional Moscarda Moscarda himself believes in, which is one more Moscarda among the others and not the underwriting reality the others fall short of. The novel ends with Moscarda renouncing the project of being any of them.

I do not believe this picture in the strong form Pirandello presents it, because I think there is a sense in which one's self-conception has a kind of evidentiary privilege that the constructions of others do not - I know what I had for breakfast and what I was thinking about during my commute, and the Securitate informant does not, even if he correctly notes which café I went into and at what time. But the weak form of the Pirandellian point is, I think, what Verdery's book is most fundamentally about, and is what makes it more than a memoir of a particular Cold War indignity. The weak form is thus: there are versions of you out there, in the heads of people you barely know and in the institutional memories of organisations you may or may not have noticed paying attention, which are not your own version of you, which you cannot fully control, and which can occasionally, under particular institutional pressures, acquire a substance and an inertia that exceeds your capacity to refute. Verdery's file is a portrait of this happening at industrial scale, with the resources of a state, over a period of two decades. The figure called *Vera*, the Securitate's Verdery, the cunning Hungarian-sympathising American intelligence asset whose movements are tracked and whose photographs are taken from doorways, is not Verdery, and is not a misperception of Verdery in any straightforward sense, because the Securitate had access to a great deal of accurate information about Verdery and chose to assemble that information into a portrait that was sustained against contrary evidence for as long as the regime lasted. Vera was a coherent fiction maintained by an institution. She was also, Verdery comes eventually to realise, a *person*. Or near enough to one that it makes no difference; a stable, internally consistent, evidentially-supported character with a file and a history and a number of people whose professional lives were spent attending to her. That this character was not the same person as the one who had been writing in a notebook in a Transylvanian kitchen does not, on inspection, mean that one of these people was real and the other was not. It means there were two characters, and one of them happened to share Verdery's body.

Reading the file, Verdery encounters not just *Vera* but her own informants. Many of the entries in her file were produced by people she knew well and had liked, like her landlady, her interpreter, fellow villagers, and in some cases close friends. The second half of the book consists in part of her seeking these people out, after the regime has fallen, and asking them what they had done and why. Some refuse to discuss it. Some are defensive. Some are matter-of-fact in the way of people for whom what they did at the time was the ordinary cost of keeping their families intact, and who do not now propose to apologise for it. The figure who emerges most clearly from these encounters is not the informant as a moral type but the informant as a person who had, like Verdery, been multiple. The friend who reported on her was not pretending to be a friend as she was indeed being a friend, and also, on Tuesday mornings, being someone else, in another room, for a different audience, with no particular sense of conflict between the two. The single self of the moralist's account, which can either be a genuine friend or a betrayer but not both, is not the self that was actually doing the work. The actual self was several, and the several did not need to be reconciled in order to function. They functioned by *not* being reconciled.

The complicity question and the Pirandello question turn out, in Verdery's hands, to be the same question. The reason it is hard for the honest fieldworker to dismiss the structural-complicity charge by appealing to her own intentions is that her own intentions are not the only thing the work is doing. The work is also being read, filed, used, repurposed, paid for, and embedded in institutional structures that have their own purposes, and the fieldworker's intentions are a comparatively small part of the larger machine. To insist on the priority of the intentions is to insist on the version of the self that the fieldworker recognises in the mirror, and to dismiss the version that exists in the funder's grant report, the publisher's marketing copy, the foreign-policy think tank's secondary citation, and yes, the secret-police file. But the dismissed versions are not nothing. They are also doing work in the world. They have substance and inertia. They have, in some cases, more institutional weight than the version in the mirror. The fieldworker who insists on her own innocence is, in this sense, choosing one of her selves and refusing the others, and the refusal does not make the others go away.

I take this to be the actual lesson of Verdery's book, and the reason it is a more important book than its archival-curiosity premise might suggest. The Securitate were unjust to her in many specific ways. They had her followed; they intercepted her mail; they coerced her friends; they constructed a false biography that was used to determine, on at least one occasion, whether she could renew her visa. None of this is in question. What is in question is whether the response to such injustice is to insist, against the construction, on one's *real* self - the one true Verdery, the actual person, the underlying reality of which the file was a distorting representation. Verdery, by the end of the book, has more or less concluded that this response is not available, not because the Securitate were correct but because the picture of the self in which one has a singular real version that institutions can either represent accurately or fail to represent accurately is, as a picture, not how selves actually work. There were several Verderys. *Vera* was one of them. The Verdery who had liked her landlady and had been liked back was another. The Verdery who had taken funding from American institutions whose interest in her work was not always identical to hers was a third. The Verdery who had written *Transylvanian Villagers* and the Verdery who had been the subject of a three-thousand-page surveillance file were not the same person and did not need to be reconciled into a single person in order for both of them to have been real.

This is a Pirandellian conclusion and it is also, I want to suggest, the right conclusion to reach about the complicity question. The honest answer to *was she compromised by the discipline's history?* is not yes and not no but that there were several of her, and one of them was, and one of them was not, and the question of which is the *real* answer is a question that depends on which Verdery you are asking, and there is no neutral vantage from which the asking can be done. This is, I am aware, an unsatisfying answer if one wants a verdict; it is also, I think, the answer that survives looking at the situation honestly. The fieldworker who insists on her innocence is not lying, but she is selecting; the fieldworker who concedes complicity is not confessing, but she is selecting differently; and the work of being a person in such a situation is the work of holding the various selections open without pretending that any of them resolves into a single truth. Verdery is good at this. The book is good at it because she is.

I want to end with a small observation about what Verdery does not do, which is also revealing. She does not write the book as a victim, and she does not write it as a confession. She writes it as an ethnography, of the Securitate, of late socialist Romania, of the small economy of betrayal that allowed one of the most thorough surveillance regimes in Europe to function on a comparatively modest budget. The discipline that the Securitate suspected her of weaponising against the regime turns out to be the discipline she eventually uses to write the regime's social history from the inside, with the affection and the analytic patience that she had originally trained on the villages it made it impossible for her to keep visiting. *My Life as a Spy* is, among other things, the ethnography that should have been written about the Securitate by someone of Verdery's training, and was not, because the Securitate would not have permitted it. That she has now written it, using the file the Securitate prepared as her primary source, is a small piece of disciplinary justice that is also, characteristically, the kind of justice that takes thirty years and the collapse of an entire political system to deliver. The discipline turns out to have been useful for the thing it is supposed to be useful for, in the end. It just needed the regime that suspected it to fall first.
