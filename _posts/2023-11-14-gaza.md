### Note
*This was written several months into the bombardment of Gaza, with casualty figures and conditions current to that period. The figures have, in the time since, more than tripled. The conditions have worsened in ways I could not describe then and still cannot bring myself to describe in any detail now, because the detail is the point at which language stops being an instrument and starts being a participant. What follows is from the earlier moment. The reader should know that the more recent moment is worse.*


## I.

I attended an American school in Doha. A great many of my classmates were Palestinian, in the sense that their parents had carried them out of one or another of the dispossessions that have followed each other across the past seventy-five years and that had ended in Doha because Doha was where the visas ran. Their nationalities, on the documents, varied from Jordanian, Lebanese, Egyptian, occasionally American, by the slow accident of professional migration. Asked where they were from, in the lunch room or on the playground, they said *Palestinian* without hesitation. The place they meant was not a place any of them had ever lived, because none of them had ever been allowed to. Some had grandparents who remembered. Some had only the photographs, the oral histories, and the keys their families kept against the day of a return that everyone professionally managing the situation knows will never be permitted. The keys were not metaphors.

I want to begin here because the school is the part of this story that is mine. The rest of the story belongs to people I have never met, who have been under bombs for several weeks now and who are continuing to be under bombs as I write this sentence. What I have access to is the small, specific fact of having sat next to those particular children, having read the same textbooks they read about democratic values and the rule of law and the inviolability of human rights, and having watched, in the years since, as the world those textbooks described has revealed itself to be, on this particular question, an ongoing performance whose audience does not include the children whose lives are the price of admission.

This essay is about this performance. I have been carrying it for some time and I cannot any longer carry it without writing it down.

## II.

The acronym now in use among the medical personnel still working in the territory is WCNSF, which stands for *Wounded Child, No Surviving Family*. Such an acronym should not exist. It has had to be invented, over the past several weeks, because the population of children to whom it applies has become large enough to require shorthand.

I keep stopping when I try to write about this, because the sentence I have just written is true, and the truth of it is the kind of truth that I do not know how to leave on the page without first sitting with it. I will go on.

What is happening in Gaza, as I write, is a genocide. I use the word in its legal sense, with reference to the Genocide Convention and its enumerated criteria, and not as a rhetorical intensifier. The intent has been declared in public by the relevant officials. The Israeli prime minister has invoked Amalek, which in the relevant biblical passage commands the killing of every man, woman, infant, and animal of the targeted people, and he has done so in a televised address as the bombs were falling. The Israeli defence minister has described the population being bombed as *human animals*. The Israeli president has said there are no innocents, that the whole population is responsible. Cabinet ministers have called for nuclear weapons to be used. The finance minister has welcomed the *voluntary emigration* of Palestinians to other countries, which is not what voluntary emigration looks like when the country one is being voluntarily emigrated from is being bombed flat.

The acts have followed the intent. Eleven thousand Palestinians have been killed at the time of this writing, the great majority of them civilians, the great majority of those women and children. Thirty thousand have been wounded. Hospitals have been bombed. Schools, refugee camps, bakeries have been bombed. Water cut off. Electricity cut off. Fuel cut off. Food convoys blocked at the border. The acts and the intent have been documented to a standard that, in any other case, would have produced an immediate International Criminal Court referral and a UN Security Council resolution. They have not, in this case, for reasons I will come to.

## III.

The narrative under which the killing has been justified to Western audiences begins on the seventh of October, which is the date on which Hamas militants breached the wall that has surrounded Gaza for the past sixteen years and killed approximately twelve hundred Israelis, including a substantial number of civilians, and took roughly two hundred and forty hostages. This was a serious crime and the people who committed it were responsible for it, and the families of the people they killed have a grievance against them that nothing I am about to write should be read as minimising.

What I want to write about is the way the seventh of October has been used, in the weeks since, as a chronological starting point that obscures the seventy-five years preceding it.

The seventy-five years include the expulsion, in 1948, of approximately seven hundred and fifty thousand Palestinians from the homes they had lived in for generations. They include the seizure of the West Bank and Gaza in 1967 and the occupation that has continued since. The settlements built in violation of the Fourth Geneva Convention. The wall built in violation of the ICJ's 2004 advisory opinion. The system of separate roads, separate legal codes, separate rights for the Israeli and Palestinian populations of the same territory that B'Tselem and Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International have, in carefully documented reports, described as apartheid. They include the siege of Gaza, beginning in 2007, which has cut the territory off from the outside world, has restricted the entry of food and fuel and medical supplies, has produced the highest unemployment rate of any economy in the world, and which the present UN Secretary-General has described as the largest open-air prison in the world. They include the periodic bombing campaigns Israeli officials have referred to internally as *mowing the lawn*. They include the Great March of Return in 2018 and 2019, in which unarmed protesters approaching the fence were shot by snipers in their thousands, with the wounds calibrated, on the testimony of one of the snipers later interviewed, to maim rather than to kill, in order to produce the maximum disabling effect with the minimum political cost.

This is the *before* that the *after* of the seventh of October has been used to obscure. The narrative under which the seventh of October becomes the *beginning* of the story is a narrative that requires the seventy-five years to disappear, and that requires the people who broke the wall to be characterised not as the third generation of a population imprisoned in conditions widely described as concentrationary by the late Israeli historian Baruch Kimmerling, but as the latest expression of a primal antisemitism that has no causes external to itself and that therefore requires no historical context for its containment. The narrative is doing very specific rhetorical work in service of a very specific outcome, which is the licensing of mass violence against a population whose history the audience has been encouraged to forget.

You can see the work being done in the headlines. Palestinians *die*; Israelis are *killed*. Palestinian deaths are aggregated as numbers; Israeli deaths are personalised with names, photographs, biographies. Palestinian sources for casualty figures are described as *Hamas-controlled* and treated as untrustworthy; Israeli sources are repeated without qualification, including the claims that have subsequently been disproved, such as the *forty beheaded babies* claim that circulated in the first days, was repeated by the President of the United States as if he had personally seen the relevant intelligence, and turned out to be an invention. The Israeli former permanent representative to the UN, Dore Gold, made the underlying logic unusually explicit when he said, in response to international expressions of concern over the Palestinian death toll: *I am very puzzled about the constant concern which the world is showing for the Palestinian people, for these horrible inhuman animals who have done the worst atrocities that this century has seen.*  The hospital strike at al-Ahli was reported with a careful even-handedness that gave equal weight to the Israeli account and to the Gazan account; the next day this even-handedness was abandoned in favour of the Israeli account; the forensic investigations conducted since have been substantially less favourable to the Israeli account than the coverage of that second day implied. The point is not that any one story got reported wrong. The point is that an Israeli denial is decisive evidence and a Palestinian claim is as a partisan position requiring corroboration.

## IV.

I want to spend a section on the question that I think is the deepest one the present moment has to teach, which is how the killing of children has been made conceptually possible to populations whose moral tradition forbids it.

The Western moral tradition, in its various religious and secular forms, contains an essentially exceptionless prohibition on the killing of children. This is one of the very few moral claims that is held with something like cross-cultural and cross-historical consistency. Even the practitioners of the most brutal forms of warfare have, in most periods, recognised that the killing of children requires special justification, and have, in most periods, attempted to provide such justification rather than openly defend the practice. The prohibition is not absolute (there have been periods and cultures in which it has been violated openly) but it is the closest thing we have to a moral universal, and it is the prohibition that the present operations are most obviously violating.

So how is this being made acceptable to populations that hold the prohibition?

The answer, I think, is that the killing is not being made acceptable as the killing of children. It is being made acceptable through a prior conceptual operation in which the bodies of the children being killed are conceptually stripped of the properties that would qualify them as falling under the prohibition. The children are no longer, on this operation, simply children. They are *human shields*, whose deaths are the responsibility of those who placed them in the line of fire. They are *future terrorists*, whose deaths are pre-emptive and therefore necessary. They are *the children of darkness*, in the phrase the Israeli prime minister used in one of his early addresses, whose deaths are part of a metaphysical struggle in which the ordinary categories of moral evaluation do not apply. They are the offspring of *human animals*, in the defence minister's phrase, and the offspring of human animals are not, on the relevant conceptual grid, fully children in the sense that the prohibition is concerned with.

The prohibition remains in force. It has simply been determined, in advance of any specific case, that the children being killed are not the kind of children the prohibition was meant to protect.

This is the move I want to draw your attention to, because it is rarely made explicit and it is doing all the philosophical work. The general norm, *do not kill children*, is preserved, which stands for some kind of ban on the part of persons in authority and holds within it some kind of threat, a perfectly understood threat that if you disobey this order dire consequences may follow. The specific killings, of *these* children, in *this* place, by *these* forces, are made consistent with the general norm by a prior operation that removes the specific children from the class to which the general norm applies. It is performed in the language of shields and future combatants and the consequences of failure to evacuate, and in the more direct language of *children of darkness* and *human animals* and *not really civilians*. It does not require anyone to deny the moral importance of children in general, as a class. It requires only that the speaker treat the specific children being killed as belonging to a category that, on inspection, the general norm was never really about.

Judith Butler has called this *the differential allocation of grievability*. The framework works by determining, in advance of any specific case, which lives count as the kind of lives whose loss can be grieved as a moral catastrophe and which lives count as the kind of lives whose loss is simply a fact about the world. The grievable lives are the lives the framework was built to protect; the ungrievable lives are the lives the framework was built to make killable without disturbance to its operation. The determination is not made on the basis of the lives themselves, which are in all relevant respects identical. It is made on the basis of which population the lives belong to, and which political and historical forces have determined that this population is to be the one whose losses do not matter.

The dehumanisation literature is almost monotonous on this point. The Hutu leaders called the Tutsis cockroaches, and the genocide followed. The Nazi propagandists called the Jews rats, and the Holocaust followed. The American military in Vietnam used the term gooks, and the Mere Gook Rule held that any Vietnamese could be killed without consequence, and the killing followed. The Japanese soldiers at Nanjing were instructed to think of the Chinese as something of less value than a dog or cat, and the rape of Nanjing followed. The pattern is so consistent across so many independent cases that calling it a pattern is an understatement. The first move is the dehumanising vocabulary. The second is the violence the vocabulary licenses. The third is the eventual reckoning, which arrives years or decades later, when the remaining members of the targeted population are too few to require any further accommodation and the dehumanisation is permitted to be remembered as a historical embarrassment.

The Israeli officials I quoted earlier are, on the available evidence, in the second phase. They are speaking the dehumanising vocabulary in public; they are conducting the violence the vocabulary licenses; they are doing so under the cover of a state that has the diplomatic and military backing of the most powerful country in the world; and they are not being stopped because the powerful country has decided not to stop them.

I want to insist that this is not a paranoid reading. It is the reading the relevant officials have themselves provided when they have spoken plainly. *Children of darkness* is the phrase. *Human animals* is the phrase. *Not really civilians* is the phrase. The killings have followed the language. To see clearly what is happening requires only that one stop participating in the distortion of language. That is the small thing one can do. It is not enough, but it is what is available.

## V.

A brief word on the political conditions, because they are part of the story and because the unfreedom of the present moment is not a peripheral feature.

In the UK, the Home Secretary has described the protests against the bombing, which have brought hundreds of thousands of people into the streets of London, organised by coalitions including substantial Jewish participation, as *hate marches*. In the US, the political atmosphere has reached a level of repressive intensity several commentators have compared, with some justice, to the McCarthyite period. Anti-Zionist Jewish student organisations have been suspended from campuses for organising protests. Journalists at major outlets have been disciplined or terminated for social-media posts. Wall Street firms have circulated lists of names of student activists they consider unemployable, in a practice that resembles, almost exactly, the blacklisting of suspected communists in the 1950s. In Germany, the Palestinian flag has been banned from public display in several cities. The keffiyeh has been banned in some schools. Pro-Palestinian demonstrations have been prohibited in advance. The principle that the German state has been organised around since 1945 is *never again*; the principle is now being administered as *never again to certain populations*, with the others left as they always were.

The point I want to draw out is not that Western liberal societies are hypocritical, although they are. The point is that the suppression of speech and political organisation around Palestine, in the centres of imperial power, is a condition of the violence in Gaza, in the sense that the violence requires this suppression in order to continue at the scale and with the impunity that it currently does. If the political opposition were able to mobilise without the costs that have been imposed on it (the loss of jobs, the loss of campus standing, the formal censure, the police repression), the calculations the relevant governments are making about the costs of continued support for the bombing would tilt further toward stopping it. The suppression is what is preventing this.


## VI.

I want to return to the school, because the structural argument I have just made has a personal correlative that I want to put on the page.

The education we received was, in the relevant respects, indistinguishable from the education a child of similar age and class would have received in a private school in London or Boston. The reading lists were the same. The historical narratives were the same. The values being inculcated were the same - democracy, the rule of law, human rights, the inviolable dignity of the person. The teachers were largely American or British, with the standard liberal commitments. The curriculum was, on its own self-presentation, universal. The values it was teaching were values for everyone.

The Palestinian children in those classrooms were being taught, every day, that their lives mattered exactly as much as the lives of any other human beings; that they had inalienable rights; that the political order was supposed to recognise and protect those rights; that the long history of violence against people like them was understood to be a moral catastrophe and was now, in the post-war period, structurally prevented by the institutions that had been built for that purpose.

The education was not lying, exactly. The values it was teaching are values I still hold and would still teach. What it was doing was failing to mention that the values, as currently administered, did not in fact apply to the children being taught them. The children would, on returning home from school, have access to news that contradicted the curriculum at every relevant point. Their cousins were being shot at the fence. Their family homes had been demolished and built over by settlements. Their applications to visit their own ancestral villages had been denied. The institutions that the curriculum described as protecting human dignity were the same institutions that, in the relevant cases, were declining to protect their dignity. The rights the curriculum described as inalienable were, in their case, alienable on the explicit basis of their being who they were. The contradiction between the curriculum and the world was, is, present in every newspaper. It is present in every conversation with their grandparents. It is present in the keys.

The children adapted to this contradiction in the way that children always adapt to contradictions of this kind, which is by acquiring an early and durable scepticism about the values they were being taught while continuing to perform the formal exercises that the curriculum required of them. The values internalised in their incomplete form, as values that *could* be universal, that *should* be universal, that are spoken of as universal, but that are in practice administered selectively and that the people on the wrong end of the selection are required, by the rules of polite society, to pretend they have not noticed.

What the present moment has done, for me and for many others I know, is end the polite pretence. The pretence was sustainable as long as the gap between the curriculum and the world could be described as an imperfect implementation of values that were nonetheless real. The values, as administered, were never universal and were never going to be; that the population being killed in Gaza is being killed in the open, with the explicit support of the institutions whose foundational documents forbid exactly this killing, and that the institutions are not going to stop. This has happened in front of us. It has happened with the relevant officials saying, in public, what they intend to do, and then doing it, and then saying, after they have done it, that they will do more of it.


## VII.

I do not know what to do with this, except to refuse to participate in the pretence any longer.

The refusal does not save anyone in Gaza. I am aware that it does not save anyone in Gaza. The refusal is not a substitute for the political action that would save people in Gaza, which has not been mobilised in any of the centres of power where it would have effect, and which I have no special access to mobilise. The refusal is the smallest possible thing one can do, which is to say clearly what one has seen, in the language one has available, in the recognition that the saying is a kind of record-keeping rather than a kind of intervention.

The record will exist. The people who continued to speak the language of human rights as if it had operative force on this question, while the relevant populations were being killed, will be remembered as having done so. The people who said clearly what was happening will be remembered for that too. The remembering does not save anyone.

The Palestinian classmates I sat next to in school have spent their entire lives doing this work, the work of refusing the polite pretence, of saying clearly what is being done, of carrying the keys. They have done it with a steadiness that the rest of us have not had to develop, because we have not had to. We are now being asked to develop it. The ones of us who do not are choosing not to. 

I will end here, because the essay has gone on long enough and because there is nothing further I have to say that would not be a repetition. The children are still being killed as I write this. They will still be killed when you read it. The acronym WCNSF will continue to apply to a growing number of them. The institutions will continue to express concern. The bombs will continue to be supplied. The casualty count will continue to climb. I am writing this anyway, because the writing is what I have, and because the recording matters even when nothing is being prevented by the recording, and because the children deserve to be witnessed by someone who is willing to call what is being done to them by its name.

The name is genocide. I have used it in this essay for that reason. I will continue to use it for that reason, and I will continue to be willing to defend my use of it against the various reasons offered for not using it, and I will note that the people offering those reasons are mostly not the people whose children are dying.

I will note this last fact too, and then I will stop.
