For most of the English-speaking world Georges Simenon is the man who wrote the Maigret novels. There are seventy-five of them, they have been continuously in print in one translation or another since the 1930s, they have been adapted for British television at intervals so regular that any actor of a certain age and a certain stillness can expect to be offered the part eventually, and they are, on the whole, rather good. They are also, if one has read the rest of Simenon, an evasion. The detective novels were what he wrote to keep the lights on; the books he wrote when the lights were on were the *romans durs*, the so-called hard novels, of which there are something between a hundred and a hundred and fifty depending on how one counts, and which are almost entirely unread in English outside a small constituency of admirers who tend to write about them with the slightly defensive intensity of a cult. The reissue of *Monsieur Monde Vanishes* by NYRB Classics, in Jean Stewart's 1967 translation, is the latest of several attempts to address this, and it is welcome, and it is also unlikely on its own to change very much, because the basic problem of Simenon's English reception is structural rather than promotional. We have decided what kind of writer he is, and the *romans durs* are not it.

What kind of writer he is, on the standard account, is a craftsman. A formidable producer of competent prose at industrial scales, a master of atmosphere within a defined genre, a writer whose books are reliable in the way that a kitchen knife is reliable. The Maigret novels reward this account because they were designed to. They have plots that resolve, they have a recurring detective who is humane and patient and not too clever, they have weather and food and the kind of incidental social observation that gives the reader the pleasant impression of having visited France. André Gide called Simenon "the greatest of all, the most genuinely a novelist that we have today in our literature," but Gide was reading the *romans durs*, not the Maigrets, and the English-speaking world has spent eighty years assuming Gide was being polite.

*Monsieur Monde Vanishes* is one of them. It was written in 1944, published in 1945 as *La Fuite de Monsieur Monde*, and concerns a Parisian businessman of forty-eight who, on the morning after his birthday, withdraws three hundred thousand francs from the family firm and gets on a train to Marseille without telling anyone. He does this for no reason. The novel is unusually scrupulous about the no-reason, and the scruple is the point insofar as this is not a midlife crisis novel, it is not a hidden-trauma novel, it is not a novel in which Monde turns out to have been carrying a secret that would, if revealed, organise his behaviour into a familiar shape. The reason for the flight is simply that the flight occurs. The narrative gives no further account, and most of the critical literature on the book has spent the better part of seventy years trying to supply one anyway, because we are extremely uncomfortable with the alternative.

The alternative, which is what I take Simenon to be advancing, is that a great deal of human action proceeds without the kind of inner architecture that the realist novel was developed to dramatise. There is no scene in which Monde decides. There is no scene in which Monde is shown to have been on the verge of deciding for some time. There is, instead, a man who gets up one morning and acts, and whose subsequent behaviour is described from a vantage point that is meticulously refused entry to whatever is going on behind his eyes. Simenon writes about him from the outside. We see what Monde sees, what Monde does, what Monde notices about the rooms he is in; we are not told what Monde thinks, except in the rare moments when Monde himself does not know what Monde thinks and the narrator records the not-knowing as a fact about the surface. This is a deliberate technical choice and it is the achievement of the novel, and it is also the reason the novel cannot be filed under the rubric the Maigrets fit into. The Maigrets are about a man whose interior is benign and accessible; the *romans durs* are about characters whose interiors are either inaccessible or, more disturbingly, not really there.

The standard move at this point, in a piece of criticism about Simenon, is to invoke Camus and to say that Monde is a kind of Meursault. This is partially right and mostly misleading. Meursault is opaque to others but quite clear to himself; he tells the reader what he sees and feels with a flatness that is unmistakeably a chosen register, and the reader understands, by the end of *L’Étranger*, that Meursault has a position even if it is one Camus wants us to find unsettling. Monde has no position. The flatness in *Monsieur Monde Vanishes* is not the flatness of an interior that has been stripped to its essentials, it is the flatness of an exterior described by a narrator who declines to assert that there is an interior at all. Camus is doing existentialism. Simenon is doing something else, for which we do not really have a name in English-language criticism, and which I would call the literature of the absent self if I thought the phrase would survive the trip from Paris.

The novel begins with Madame Monde at the police station, reporting her husband's disappearance, and the opening pages are masterful in a particular way. We are introduced to Monde through his wife's account of him, which is the account of a man so thoroughly defined by his routines that his absence is detectable primarily as the failure of those routines to be performed. He has not come home for dinner. He has not been at the office. He has not made the telephone calls he reliably makes at certain hours. The wife is concerned and not because she particularly misses him but because the machine of his life has stopped emitting its ordinary sounds, and she does not know how to interpret this. This is comic and it is also the establishing shot for the entire book, wherein Monde exists, for the people around him, as a pattern of behaviours, and the pattern is what they have been responding to all along. An external stimuli. When Monde flees, what flees is a vacated position.

Simenon then tracks Monde, with the camera at his shoulder, through the sequence of small actions that constitute his flight. The famous scene in the Paris hotel where he wraps the stolen money in a ball of string is a piece of psychological evidence presented under bond, in that it is shown very expressly rather than glossed. Monde has been a businessman his entire life and has never until this moment had to handle large amounts of cash without an institutional framework for doing so. He fumbles and produces the wrong gestures. He behaves with the conspicuous guilt of a man who has never broken a rule in his life and is now uncertain which rules he is breaking. None of this is interpreted for us. The narrator does not say that Monde is guilty, or that Monde is afraid, or that Monde is becoming aware of how unfit for this he is. The narrator merely describes the string and we draw the conclusions. This is the novel's basic procedure and it is sustained for two hundred pages without breaking.

The technique is, when one notices it, almost shocking in how completely it differs from what we expect from a serious twentieth-century novel. The whole apparatus of free indirect style, which is the great realist invention for getting the reader inside a character without the awkwardness of explicit interior monologue, is simply absent. So is interior monologue itself, in the Joyce sense; so is the sustained psychological gloss that Henry James spent a career refining; so is the depth-psychological apparatus that allows a novelist like Updike or Bellow to go into a character's history and produce, on demand, the formative episode that explains the present. Simenon will not give us any of this. He gives us the room, and the man in the room, and the time of day, and the weather, and the stained menu the man is reading, and that is the entirety of the novel. The reader who expects the curtain to be drawn back and the inner life to be revealed will wait through the entire book, and the curtain will not be drawn, and the disappointment, if one experiences it as disappointment, is in fact the reader's encounter with what the novel is actually about. There is no curtain. There is no inner life of the kind we have been trained to look for. There is a man, and a series of rooms, and a sequence of actions, and the gradual recognition by the reader that this might be what some lives, perhaps most lives, perhaps to a greater or lesser degree all lives, actually consist of.

The middle of the novel takes Monde through Marseille and then to Nice, where he attaches himself, in a manner that the novel deliberately leaves unmotivated, to a woman called Julie who has tried to kill herself in the next hotel room. He saves her, more or less by reflex, and they end up travelling together as a kind of accidental couple, taking jobs at the Monico, a Niçois nightclub of the lower sort, where Monde becomes a steward in a pantry that smells of tinned foie gras and Julie becomes one of the hostesses whose function is to ensure that the male clientele drinks champagne while remaining in a state of permanent erotic deferral. The Monico section is, on one level, a piece of period social observation of the kind Simenon was very good at - the mechanics of the racket, the rigged games, the careful temporal management by which the clocks are removed and the music is staggered so that the customers cannot tell how long they have been losing money - and on another level it is the novel's most concentrated expression of its central idea, which is that Monde's flight from his bourgeois life has delivered him not into freedom but into a different system, equally rigged, equally administered, equally dependent on the steady extraction of value from people who have agreed not to notice they are being extracted from. The escape was in fact mere relocation.

This is the point at which the novel announces that it has been a different kind of book all along than the one it appeared to be. The first hundred pages read as the story of a man's flight; the second hundred pages reveal that the flight was an illusion, in the sense that what Monde fled was not specific to his Paris life but is general to the human social arrangement, and that Monde would have encountered some version of it wherever he went. The bourgeois household in the Rue Ballu and the gambling den in Nice are not opposites. They are variations on a single theme, which is that human beings construct elaborate machineries for the purpose of not noticing what they are doing, and that one cannot exit these machineries by walking out of one of them, because the next one is already waiting, and the next, and the next. This is the book's bleakness, and it is more sustained and more thorough than anything in Camus, because it is not making a philosophical argument about the absurd and it is rather making an anthropological observation about the species, and it is making it through the slow accumulation of physical detail in particular rooms.

The Thérèse episode - Monde's first wife, a morphine addict, turns up in Nice and he becomes her keeper for a while - is the book's most explicit demonstration of this point. Monde fled his second marriage. He is now, by accident, taking responsibility for the woman from his first marriage, in circumstances if anything more degrading than the ones he left. He does this, as he does everything in the novel, without commentary and apparently without choice. The reflex of the provider has outlived the role of the provider. He hands over money. He procures the morphine. He behaves, in a Niçois hotel room with a strange woman from his past, exactly as he would have behaved in his Paris office with the family firm; the same competence, the same patience, the same low-grade resentment at having been once again positioned as the responsible party. The novel is asking us to consider whether what we call character is anything more than a set of such reflexes, deployed in whatever environment one happens to be in.

The ending is the most controversial thing in the book, and it is also where the case for Simenon's seriousness as a novelist becomes hardest to refuse. Monde returns to Paris. He delivers Thérèse to a doctor. He resumes his life on the Rue Ballu - the firm, the wife, the children, the suit, the pipe, the desk - and he resumes it with no apparent difficulty and no apparent revelation. His wife sniffles theatrically. His son faints at the sight of him. The doctor suggests he visit Thérèse, who is recovering and labouring under the impression that he is in Nice waiting for her, and Monde declines: *what’s the use?* The novel ends with Monde back in his old position, performing his old functions, with the only difference being that he now sees them for what they are and has surrendered the energy required to be troubled by the seeing.

This is, on a quick reading, deflating. The hero has been on a journey and the journey has produced no transformation; the rebellion has fizzled; the bourgeois has been reabsorbed into the bourgeoisie. A great deal of criticism has wanted to read the ending as a defeat, or as a failure of nerve on Simenon's part, or as the moment at which the novel reveals itself to have been a less serious enterprise than it appeared. I think this reading is wrong, and I think the wrongness is diagnostic of how thoroughly we have internalised the conventions the novel is trying to refuse. The ending is not a defeat. It is the formal completion of the argument the book has been making throughout. There was nothing to be transformed. There was no inner Monde whose liberation could have been achieved by leaving the Rue Ballu, because the inner Monde was, all along, a function of the position Monde occupied, and once one understands this there is no particular reason not to occupy the position again. The position is as good as any other position. The flight and the return are not opposites; they are episodes in a life that has stopped pretending to be the kind of life that has a shape.

This is a hard thing for a novel to do and an even harder thing for a novel to do without descending into nihilism or affectation, and Simenon manages it. The reason he manages it is that he never overstates the case. The novel does not announce, at any point, that life is meaningless or that the self is a fiction or that bourgeois existence is a trap. It simply describes a man, and the rooms he is in, and the things he does, and lets the description carry the weight that a more conventional novelist would have placed on commentary. The technique is what the *romans durs* are for. It is what Simenon developed across roughly forty years and a hundred and fifty books, refining it into something that no other novelist of the period was doing at the same level of consistency and that no novelist since has quite reproduced. Patricia Highsmith is the closest analogue in English, and Highsmith herself acknowledged the debt, but Highsmith's psychopaths have the courtesy of being lurid, where Simenon's bourgeois are not even that. They are simply present. They do things. The doings accumulate. A book happens.

What we have lost, by reading only the Maigrets, is an entire body of work that does this, over and over, in different settings, with different protagonists, but with the same fundamental commitment to the externalised description of selves that may or may not have interiors and that the novel is not going to commit either way on. It is a peculiarly modern body of work, and it is also peculiarly resistant to the categories under which modern literature is usually shelved. Simenon is not a modernist; he has none of the formal experimentation, none of the language-as-such preoccupation, none of the alienation effects. He is not a realist either, in the nineteenth-century sense; the social fabric in his novels is sketched rather than embroidered, and he has no interest in the kind of comprehensive social portraiture Balzac was doing. He is not a noir writer, despite the genre proximity; the noir tradition is built on conspiracies and crimes, and Simenon's *romans durs* are mostly built on people doing slightly inexplicable things to one another in small towns. He is sui generis, which is part of the problem, because critics need categories and writers without categories tend to drift to the back of the shelf.

The NYRB reissue of *Monsieur Monde Vanishes* is part of a long, intermittent project to bring the *romans durs* back into English-language circulation, and it deserves to succeed. Whether it will is unclear. The reader who comes to the book expecting another Maigret will be disappointed; the reader who comes to it expecting the more familiar varieties of literary seriousness will be disappointed in the opposite direction; the reader who comes to it with no expectations and a willingness to sit with what Simenon is actually doing will, I think, be left with the sense of having read something quite unlike anything else in twentieth-century European fiction, and quite unlike anything that has been written since. That this writer has been, for eighty years, primarily known to English readers as the author of pleasant detective novels about a Parisian commissioner is a piece of literary-historical injustice on a scale that the gradual reissue of the *romans durs* is unlikely to correct in our lifetime. It is, however, a place to start. *Monsieur Monde Vanishes* is the right place to start. The man on the train to Marseille is, in some respects, the most fully realised character in postwar French fiction, and he is so by virtue of being almost not realised at all.
