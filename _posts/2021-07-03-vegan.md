title: "Why do people hate vegans?"
permalink: /vegan

There is a small social event in my memory (I am being vague about it because being specific would identify people I do not want to identify) at which I was asked, in the way one is always asked, why my plate was missing the meat. I gave the answer I usually give, which is that I would rather not eat other animals. The first response was approving. Someone said something about how good it was that I was looking after my health. I corrected this gently. It was not a health thing, I said; I think it is wrong to kill them when one does not have to. The temperature of the table changed. Within a few minutes I had been called, in escalating order, a hippie, a fanatic, and, when I tried to defuse the situation by reaching for a religious text that I thought might at least give us shared ground, a heretic.

I have been thinking about that escalation for a while, and the thing I want to write about here is not the escalation itself, which is unremarkable, but a feature of it that took me some time to appreciate. The people at that table would, almost to a person, have agreed with most of what I would have said about industrial animal agriculture if they had been asked about it in any other setting. They would have agreed that factory farms are bad. They would have agreed that the conditions in which most of the meat they eat is produced are, when described in any detail, indefensible. Several of them had, in other conversations, mentioned trying to eat less meat. One had stopped eating red meat some years earlier on what she described as broadly ethical grounds. The hostility I encountered was not, in any straightforward sense, hostility from people who disagreed with my position. It was hostility from people who, on most of the relevant component claims, agreed with it.

The concession most people will make, in my experience, runs roughly like this: yes, factory farming is bad; yes, the conditions are indefensible; yes, I would prefer not to think about them in detail; no, I have not stopped eating meat, and I am not going to. This is a coherent position. It is not the position I hold, but I want to take it seriously as a position that a reflective person can occupy, because most reflective people I know in fact occupy it, and because failing to take it seriously is the most reliable way to misunderstand what is happening when these conversations go badly. The position is not *factory farming is fine*. The position is *factory farming is bad and I am going to keep participating in it*. 

I want to suggest that the reflexive hostility vegans encounter is not, primarily, the hostility of people who think they are right. It is the hostility of people who suspect they are wrong, who have decided not to act on the suspicion, and who experience the presence of someone who has acted on it as a kind of unwanted invoice. The clearest empirical evidence I know of for this is a pair of studies by Julia Minson and Benoît Monin, published in 2012, which I have been thinking about ever since I first read them. They asked meat-eaters to free-associate with the word *vegetarian*, and almost half produced negative terms. The negativity correlated with how morally superior the meat-eaters expected the vegetarians to feel, before any vegetarian had said anything. In the second study, they primed half the participants to think about being morally judged before they rated the vegetarians, and the primed group rated them lower. The hostility, in other words, was a defensive response to *anticipated* moral judgment, calibrated to how much judgment the meat-eater expected to receive. It had nothing to do with anything any vegetarian had actually said or done in the experimental setting. The vegetarian did not need to moralise in order to be experienced as moralising. The mere fact of the vegetarian, holding the position the vegetarian was known to hold, was sufficient to trigger the defensive response. The response was, on the part of the meat-eater, an entirely sincere reaction to a felt threat that no vegetarian was actually making.

I think this is roughly right and I think it explains a great deal. It explains why the dinner-party escalation has the shape it does. Health is a tolerable reason to abstain because health is about the speaker. Ethics is intolerable because ethics is, by implication, about everybody. *I do not eat meat for ethical reasons* is heard, accurately, as the first half of a sentence whose second half is *and you do*. The speaker does not need to complete the sentence; the listener completes it themselves and resents the speaker for the completion. The speaker, frequently, was not going to complete it. The speaker was going to ask for the bread. But the completion has already happened in the listener's head, and the resentment is therefore directed at a sentence the speaker did not say, in response to a judgment the speaker did not make, on behalf of a self the listener was already, before the conversation began, slightly unhappy with.

This is, I want to insist, not a stupid reaction. It is a defensive reaction, and defensive reactions are characteristic responses of moral agents in situations where they have something to defend. Once one has the basic mechanism in view, the various surface phenomena (the militant vegan construction in the British press, the dinner-table escalation, the Reddit subforums devoted to mocking vegans) become much easier to understand as variations on a more-or-less single theme. They are all technologies for managing the discomfort produced by the existence of people who are doing what one half-thinks one ought to do but is not going to do. The technologies vary by culture. The British press has the *militant* construction, which borrows the connotative force of armed insurgency and applies it to a population whose actual conduct rarely exceeds the holding of placards. The American culture wars have the *soyboy* construction, which routes the threat through anxieties about masculinity. The Arab dinner party, in my experience, has the *heretic* construction, which is the one I encountered when I reached for the religious passage and which I want to spend a moment on, because it is the version that most clearly displays the structure of the underlying defence.

When I quoted that text I was trying to defuse. I was not trying to convert anyone. The thought, naively, was that if there was a way to read the tradition as constraining what we owed to animals, that reading might produce the same conclusion I had reached on secular grounds, and then we would all be on the same side and could go back to eating. This is not how it went. The reach for the religious frame was treated as more provoking than the ethical frame had been. It produced a kind of moral alarm. I think the structure is something like this. The secular ethical claim sits at the edge of the moral order without quite contradicting it, and so can be tolerated as eccentricity, dismissed as Western fashion, treated as a personal foible. The religious claim cannot be tolerated in this way because it operates on the same plane as the moral order itself, which in this context is also a religious order. To suggest that there is a reading of the tradition that constrains animal consumption is to claim authority on the same terrain on which the practice of eating animals has its sanction. It is a hostile takeover bid for the same building, and it is resisted with the kind of force one resists hostile takeover bids with. The eccentric vegan can be patronised. The vegan with a textual argument is a heretic.

I should say, before going on, that the textual case is real and not eccentric. The hadith material on animal welfare is substantial; the question of whether industrial slaughter satisfies the conditions of *dhabīhah* is live among scholars who have looked at it carefully; the early Sufi tradition contains figures who renounced meat for reasons that look very recognisable from inside the modern argument. I am not going to litigate any of this here, partly because I am not qualified and partly because the textual case is not the point. The point is that the textual case, even raised tentatively, gets read as an attack on the tradition rather than as a question within it. That asymmetry is what I want to draw out. It is much easier to be a Muslim who eats meat without thinking about it than to be a Muslim who has thought about it and concluded otherwise; the first is unmarked, the second is marked as something close to apostasy. The moral order recognises which moves are dangerous to it and which are not, and it organises its defences accordingly.

What this lets us see is that the British morning-show treatment of vegans and the Arab dinner-party treatment of vegans are two registers of the same underlying mechanism. The British register is secular, which means it cannot call you a heretic and so calls you a militant instead. The Arab register has the older vocabulary still available and uses it. In both cases the function of the language is to place the vegan position outside the space of admissible disagreement. You are not someone with whom the moral order can have an argument. You are something the moral order can only react to. And in both cases the reaction is most violent not from the people who disagree with you most strongly but from the people whose private agreement with you is closest to the surface and most actively repressed, because those are the people for whom your existence is the most direct procedural threat. The person who has never thought about animal suffering at all is rarely hostile. The person who has thought about it for ten minutes and decided to stop thinking about it is the one who will mock you at the table. The hostility scales with the proximity of the suppressed concession.

I want to be careful about the next step, because it is the step the argument most easily overshoots. The fact that the hostility is defensive, and that it tracks a half-conceded moral discomfort, is not by itself an argument that the vegan is right about the underlying question. People defend many things they should not, in fact, give up. The structure of the defence does not adjudicate the substantive question. What it does is explain why the substantive question keeps not getting properly discussed: because the people who would be most able to discuss it usefully (the ones who already have the relevant intuitions in roughly the right place) are also the ones most heavily invested in not discussing it, because for them the discussion is most likely to end somewhere they would rather not be. The mainstream nonvegan position is not held by people who have thought about it carefully and decided meat-eating is fine. It is held, in most cases, by people who have decided not to think about it carefully, and whose defensive posture toward those who do is calibrated to keep the not-thinking sustainable. This is a description of how the position is held, not an argument that the position is wrong. But it does explain why the holders of the position respond to the existence of vegans with mockery rather than with argument. They cannot argue, because to argue would be to engage the substance, and the substance is precisely what the position has been organised around not engaging.

There is a counterversion of this argument that I want to acknowledge before I close, because I think it is the strongest version of what my interlocutors at the dinner party would have said if they had been able to articulate it. The counterversion is thus: yes, the position is held defensively; yes, the defence is calibrated to manage discomfort rather than to vindicate the underlying practice; but this is not a special problem for meat-eating. It is the structure of almost every moral compromise that adults make in order to live ordinary lives. The person who flies to see their family knows about the climate. The person who buys cheap clothes knows about the supply chain. The person who uses a smartphone knows about the cobalt mines and the suicide nets. Adult moral life consists, in part, in the management of suppressed concessions, and the people who are loudest about any one of them tend to be quietly compromised on the others. The vegan who will not shut up about the killing of billions of cows is on the iPhone made in Zhengzhou. To single out the animal meat case as uniquely revealing of moral cowardice is itself, on this view, a kind of defensive move, one that lets the vegan feel virtuous about the one practice they have abandoned while ignoring the dozen they have not.

I think this counterversion not correct (I think it is a fallacy of relative privation - non of the other issues going on are mutually exclusive with veganism). Yes, the structure is general. Yes, I am compromised on most of the other axes. Yes, my insistence on this particular axis is partly a function of the fact that it is the one I happened to take up rather than some deep claim about its priority over the others. None of that makes the eighty billion animals less unjustly killed (annually!). The generality of the mechanism is not a reason to stop attending to any particular instance of it; if anything, it is a reason to be patient with the people who are attending to instances I have not yet got round to attending to myself, and to expect, from them, the same patience back. The vegan and the climate person and the supply-chain person are all, in their different ways, doing the same thing, which is refusing to participate in one of the practices their society has organised itself around not thinking about. The mockery they all receive has the same structure, for the same reasons, and the response to the mockery should probably also have the same structure: keep doing it, do not be self-righteous about it, and try to remember that the people doing the mocking are not, mostly, your enemies. They are people who have made a different bargain with the same difficulty, and they are tired, and they would rather not have the difficulty raised at dinner. Most of the time, neither would I.

I have been vegan now for long enough that I no longer expect any particular dinner to go well. I have stopped reaching for the religious frame, because I have learned that it costs more than it is worth. I have learned to give the health answer when I am tired and the ethical answer when I am not. I do not think I am morally superior to the people I eat with; I think I am embarrassed in front of the animals, and I would rather be embarrassed in front of the people than in front of them. Whether that is the right way to feel I am genuinely not sure. But it is the actual reason, and I would rather say the actual reason than perform a reason I do not have.
