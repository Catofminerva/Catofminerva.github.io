title: "The Rise and Fall of the Postwar British State-subsidised Intelligentsia"
permalink: /intelligentsia
---  



The postwar settlement in welfare-state Britain created a generation who could go to redbrick universities on grants, work in newly-funded arts institutions, teach in expanding humanities departments and do all of this while understanding themselves as participants in a collective project of social democratisation. This was a matter of a moral ethos as much as anything else. The NHS, Education Act, nationalisation, the grant system, was a particular way of understanding what Britain was for. There was a genuine mood that the war had created a genuinely cross-class experience of collective suffering and collective purpose and which made redistribution of wealth acceptable to most. People who'd shared Anderson shelters and rationing books had a temporarily expanded sense of social solidarity, and this was institutionalised in the form of the settlement. The expansion of universities and arts funding, including the  redbrick expansion (and later the plate glass universities of the 60s, I.e. Sussex, Warwick, York) was therefore premised on the idea that cultural and intellectual life had been artificially restricted to a small class, and that widening access was a democratic good comparable to the NHS. The grammar school system, whatever its later pathologies, genuinely delivered on this for a generation. Working-class kids from Leeds and Birmingham could end up in humanities departments alongside people whose fathers had been to Oxford, and there was an institutional logic that declared this as good, correct and desirable.

The people who came through that system in the 50s and 60s—the first beneficiaries—held a consciousness of both origin and arrival. Raymond Williams, as a paradigmatic case, came from a Welsh working-class family, went to Cambridge on a scholarship, spent his career elaborating a cultural theory that attempted to hold working-class culture and high culture in productive tension rather than treating the latter as the obvious destination of the former. Culture and Society, The Long Revolution, etc. are books written by someone who genuinely inhabited both worlds and felt the friction between them as intellectually generative. Raymond was clearly attempting to theorise the relationship between culture and social structure from inside his own biography. The problem is that Williams and his generation were followed by people who inherited the institutional position without the biographical double consciousness. By the 70s and 80s you had a humanities professoriat and an arts world that was structurally middle-class (reproduced through cultural capital, credentialing, the right schools of thought and so on) but still operating inside the moral vocabulary of the postwar settlement. They still understood themselves as participants in a democratising project, but the actual social composition of their world had re-stratified. The grants were being cut, the redbrick expansion had plateaued, and the people in the arts institutions were increasingly people who could afford to do unpaid internships in London, which is to say, people who didn't need the institutional support the way the first generation had.

This created something particular about British academic-artistic culture, in which the Arts Council grant and the Oxbridge fellowship and the like had created a class of people who are materially quite comfortable while remaining ideologically committed to their own dispossession. Marxist professors’ self-flagellation becomes almost constitutive of their own comfort. You get a very specific variety of dinner party where someone's Booker-longlisted novel about working-class trauma funds a second home in Hastings. It is in essence what happens when that class reproduced itself through cultural capital rather than redistributive access. It's not that these later figures were/are hypocrites exactly (the commitment to the moral vocabulary was often genuine). But the relationship between the vocabulary and the material reality had inverted. Instead of the state funding enabling working-class access to cultural life, it was funding a professional class that talked about working-class culture while being socially removed from it. British social realism in film and theatre (the whole tradition from the kitchen sink dramas through to Ken Loach) is partly a symptom of this, that is to say, a middle-class art form that takes working-class experience as its privileged subject matter, which can be done brilliantly (Loach at his best genuinely *is* brilliant) but which also has an uncomfortable relationship to its own conditions of production.

Thatcher understood this intuitively if not analytically, which is why her attacks on the Arts Council and the BBC had genuine popular resonance in certain quarters. The charge wasn't entirely wrong; there was a self-regarding, mutually reinforcing cultural establishment that had lost connection to the publics it claimed to represent. But her solution was simply to replace public patronage with market discipline, which predictably produced a different set of distortions rather than solving the underlying problem. There is now a popular nostalgia for that postwar moment but that moment was incredibly rare, very contingent on a specific macroeconomic configuration (the long boom, cheap universities, full employment making redistribution feel costless), a specific political mood (the war's cross-class solidarity), and a specific generational experience (the Williams generation) that couldn't simply be reproduced institutionally once the conditions changed. The moral ethos outlasted the material basis for it, which is exactly the situation that produces a guilt cycle wherein an intelligentsia class became committed to a story about what they're doing that no longer quite matches what they're actually doing, and are unable to update the story without abandoning an identity built around it.
