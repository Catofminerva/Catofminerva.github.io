I should say at the outset that I am an anarchist, in a particular and not very romantic sense of the word, and that this essay is an attempt to explain how I became one against most of my own training and preferences. I did not arrive at the position through theory. I arrived at it the way most people arrive at convictions they would rather not hold, which is by repeatedly running into the same wall in different rooms. The wall, in my case, was the state, and the rooms were the various histories I was reading at the time, and the particular room in which the wall finally became unavoidable was the one containing the failed sedentarisation of the Najdi Bedouin between roughly 1912 and 1930. I want to write about that room, because I think it explains something I have not seen explained well, and because the explanation, when it finally came together for me, settled a question I had been carrying around for years.

The frame I am going to use is James Scott's, and I want to be brief about it because it has been summarised too many times. *Seeing Like a State* argues that the modern state cannot govern a population it cannot see, that most populations left to themselves are not in the kind of shape a state can see, and that the state therefore does the work of making them visible when fixing surnames, surveying plots, standardising weights, pruning languages, building cities on grids. The argument is well-known and largely correct. Scott sympathies sit not with the heroic resisters who oppose this work in the open but with the populations who avoid being seen at all. The pastoralist who happens to be elsewhere when the census comes through. The household that gives the surveyor a name nobody actually uses. The peoples Scott calls *Zomia* in his later book, who spent two thousand years living in the highlands of southeast Asia for the specific purpose of not being legible to the lowland states beneath them. These are not heroes of a revolution, but simply people who, on the available evidence, concluded that being known to a state was on balance worse for them than not being known to it, and who arranged their entire material lives around that conclusion.

The Arab nomadic pastoralists are, in Scott's terms, an almost perfect case. Scattered from the Atlas to the Hijaz, they spent the better part of a millennium evading the registers of every empire that claimed them in turn, from the Umayyad, Abbasid, Ottoman to briefly the Europeans, by the simple expedient of moving. Their social order was reciprocal and tribal, their wealth was in camels (which the state could not tax without first finding them) and their territorial claims were seasonal rather than permanent. They paid no zakat to anyone they did not personally respect. They obeyed laws when present in the cities and did not obey them in the desert. The Lehnert and Landrock photograph below of a 1910 caravan that opens this piece in my head - a long file of camels at dusk, perfectly legible as an *image* and entirely illegible as a unit of administration - is the thing I keep returning to when I try to picture what was about to be lost. If you find these people in the archives, as Scott more or less puts it, something terrible has already happened to them.

What happened to them, in the Arabian case, is the part I want to slow down on, because it is more empirically interesting than the standard narrative allows. The standard narrative, written by people sympathetic to the modern state, holds that nomadic pastoralism was a doomed mode of life, economically marginal, ecologically fragile, incompatible with the scale and speed of modernity, and that its disappearance, while regrettable in some Romantic register, was inevitable and on balance an improvement. Schools were built. Clinics were opened. Camel herds gave way to wage labour, which gave way, eventually, to the comforts of an air-conditioned villa. This is the story most Saudis under fifty have been told about their grandparents. It is also wrong in a way I think matters.

It is wrong because the early Saudi state, between roughly 1912 and 1930, made a serious and fully-resourced attempt to sedentarise the Najdi Bedouin by direct administrative effort, and the attempt failed. It did not fail at the margins; it failed catastrophically, and in failing it produced the single most destructive non-state actor the peninsula had seen in centuries. The Bedouin were not delivered to the modern world by the slow pressure of economic forces beyond anyone's control. They were finally subdued by oil money and air-conditioning and the wage relation, all of which arrived later and by accident. The thing the state actually did to them, in the period when it was trying to do something on purpose, made matters considerably worse.

The bare history is this. Ibn Saud's project from the 1910s onwards required an army he did not have the population base to recruit. The settled population of Najd was small, fractious, and not particularly martial. The nomadic population was several hundred thousand strong and was, on paper, exactly the demographic resource a state-builder needed. The problem was that they were nomads, and the additional and deeper problem was that they were, by the standards of the Wahhabi revivalist movement Ibn Saud had bound himself to, theologically suspect, too casual about prayer, too tolerant of pre-Islamic practices, too inclined to mix Islam with the inherited religious customs of the desert. The two problems were addressed simultaneously and with the same instrument. *Mutawwa'ah*, religious preachers, were dispatched into the desert to convert the Bedouin to Wahhabi orthodoxy, to teach them that the settled life of the cultivator was the morally superior one, and to organise them into agricultural settlements called *hijras* - a deliberate echo of the Prophet's migration, which loaded the move from tent to mud-brick house with the entire emotional weight of the founding event of Islam. Several hundred such villages were built. The state subsidised the housing. It financed the wells and compelled, through a mixture of pressure and incentive, the sale of the camels that were the material precondition of nomadic autonomy. By the early 1920s a population that had been mobile, reciprocal, and broadly indifferent to the religious and political claims of central authority had been reorganised into a settled, dependent, intensely religious force called the Ikhwan, the Brethren, distinguishable in the field by the white cloth bands they wore around their heads.

It worked, in the short term, in exactly the way the planners had intended it to work. The Ikhwan were the shock troops of the Saudi conquest of the Hijaz between 1924 and 1925. They were ferocious, disciplined and, by the standards of the day, extremely effective. The plan, on its own terms, succeeded. The state had taken an illegible population and made it legible, and the legible version was useful.

What the state had not anticipated, and what nobody at the time seems to have been able to anticipate, was that the new category it had created - the settled, fundamentalist, state-dependent Ikhwan warrior - would not stay where it had been put. The men who had been *made* Ikhwan by the sedentarisation project began, within a few years of the conquest, to behave as Ikhwan with a logic of their own. Having sold their camels, they were no longer autonomous and mobile, relying instead on wartime employment as Ikhwan soldiers. They turned the settlements into mosques and refused to farm. They struggled with the processes of modernity and remained largely unproductive and shunned the state’s productive forces of agricultural labour, industry and trade as heretical activities. They declared the radio, the telegraph, the automobile and tobacco to be innovations forbidden by their reading of the faith. They began attacking other Bedouin who had not been similarly converted, on the grounds that those Bedouin were now infidels. By 1927 they had begun raiding into British-protected Iraq against Ibn Saud's explicit orders, and by 1929 they had launched an open revolt against the Saudi state itself, on the grounds that Ibn Saud, having accepted modern military equipment from the British and resisted further religious purification, had himself become an apostate. The state crushed them, with British-supplied machine guns and aircraft, which is to say with exactly the modern technology the Ikhwan had declared forbidden, at the battle of Sabilla in March 1929. Some of the survivors were absorbed into what became the Saudi National Guard. Others returned to a vastly diminished pastoralism. Most of their descendants would, two generations later, be salaried employees of Aramco.

I want to draw two things out of this story. The first is that the Ikhwan were not a survival of an older religious form. They were a *creation* of the modernising state. The fanaticism for which they became famous was not what nomadic Najdi religion had looked like before the *hijras*; it was what nomadic Najdi religion looked like *after* it had been forcibly settled, theologically standardised, and rendered economically dependent on a central authority that had also taught it to think of itself as the vanguard of a universal religious truth. The category produced the people. Once produced, the people behaved according to the internal logic of the category and not according to the wishes of those who had produced them. Ian Hacking, in a different context, called this the looping effect. I think it is the single most important phenomenon for anyone who proposes to govern other people to understand, and I think it is the phenomenon that high-modernist state-builders have most consistently failed to understand, and I think the failure is not accidental but structural, because to understand it would be to abandon the project. The Saudi state could not have foreseen the Ikhwan revolt because foreseeing it would have required granting that the population it was acting on was not, in fact, raw material to be shaped, but a system whose response to being shaped would have its own emergent character, beyond the planners' control. No state, in my reading, has ever consistently granted this. They cannot. To grant it is to give up the thing that makes one a state in the first place.

The second thing, and this is the thing that took me longer to be willing to say, is that the failure of the Saudi sedentarisation project was not a special failure. It is what happened, with regional variations, in most of the comparable cases. The Soviet collectivisations, the Tanzanian villagisations, the Israeli pacification of the Negev Bedouin, the various French and British attempts in North Africa and the Levant. The pattern is consistent enough that calling it a pattern feels almost too modest. Large-scale legibility projects aimed at nomadic or otherwise illegible populations do not, in the historical record, produce the outcomes their planners expect. They produce the outcomes their planners did not think to model. They produce categories that loop, dependent populations that become uncontrollable in new ways, ecological collapses, religious movements that the original administrators would have found unrecognisable. The work of governing eventually gets done, but it gets done by other means, via markets, oil revenue, the slow attrition of demography, and what the state directly attempted is, more often than not, the part that visibly went wrong.

I do not want to overstate the position. There are many things states do that I am glad they do, and there are many functions of legibility I would not want to live without. The argument is not that all government is illegitimate, which is the kind of claim that wins debates and loses elections and convinces nobody. The argument is narrower and, I think, harder to refute. It is that the *ambition* of government, the ambition to take a population that is currently arranged according to its own logics and rearrange it according to a logic legible from the capital, has a track record sufficiently bad, across enough cases, that the burden of proof on any particular instance of it should be considerably higher than current political vocabulary allows. When a state proposes to settle a nomadic population, or to consolidate a dispersed one, or to standardise a plural one, or to bring an illegible community into the registers of the modern administrative apparatus, the appropriate first response is not enthusiasm or even cautious endorsement but a careful and historically informed no, with the understanding that the burden lies on the state to demonstrate, against the weight of every comparable precedent, that this time will be different. It almost never is.

That is what I mean when I say I am an anarchist. I do not mean that I want to abolish the post office. I mean that I have read enough of these histories to have lost the faith my education tried to give me in the basic competence of large-scale administrative reason when applied to populations that have not asked to be administered. The Ikhwan are what happens, with depressing regularity, when a clever man with a map decides that the people on the ground need to be sorted out for their own good. My great grandfather was one of the people on the ground. I am writing this from a city built on top of where his grazing routes used to be. Anyway. This is why I am an anarchist.
