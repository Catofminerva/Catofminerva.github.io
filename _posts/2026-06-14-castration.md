title: "Castration, Sublimation, Freedom"

“Castration" is what psychoanalysis chose as a name for probably its most important structural concept. While it may make you flinch, Freud meant by it something like the discovery that every child makes and every adult spends a lifetime renegotiating, the discovery that you cannot have everything, that the world does not arrange itself around your wanting, that to become a person among other persons you have to in some way give something up. There is a moment when a child runs up against this, when she becomes aware of the most basic interjection, and not in any act of cognition but simply by being impacted upon, by finding that the world contains a "No" that precedes her and will outlast her.

Put it this way. Suppose you sat down and pressed yourself with the hard questions; what reason have you to believe the world is out there, what reason to think you are not dreaming the whole thing, what could you ever say to the solipsist who insists that everything beyond his own mind is a figment of it? There is no proof of the alternative but there is something else, which is the fact that you cannot by any effort of doubt, argue away the brutal fact of a collision, between you and what you wanted, between you and what you meant to become, between you and the raw material you hoped to impose your personality upon and which, blankly, indifferently, pushed back. In this resistance, and nowhere else, the self and the not-self come into being together. Subtract the not-self and there is nothing left over to call a self; subtract the self and there is nothing for the not-self to set itself against. This is more primitive and irreducible than anything that gets built on top of it afterward or reasoned out of it later. And out of this primal disappointment of a child colliding with a ‘No’, supposedly, comes the whole of it: art, law, science, the cathedral and the symphony and the tax code.

Lacan’s account of castration was to argue that it is the price of admission to language itself. The moment you start speaking you have traded the immediate thing for the word that stands in for it, and the word is never the thing. Hegel said the word is the murder of the thing and Lacan repeated it with approval. Every speaking being is in this sense already cut. While this may sound nasty to you but consider what the alternative looks like. Consider that he uncastrated subject, if such a creature existed, would be a kind of monster of immediacy, he will have total satisfaction, no gap, no wanting, and therefore no movement, no speech, no time. Desire requires the castration. You can only want what you do not fully have and a being that lacked nothing would also, in a strict sense, do nothing.

Freud went on to explicate what he meant by ‘sublimation’ but he did so poorly and kept promising to write a proper paper about it but never did. But the idea is that the drive, blocked from its sexual aim, gets rerouted into activities that civilization rewards, and thus we get cathedrals and string quartets and number theory out of frustrated libido. On this construal sublimation is consolation, a kind of a silver medal, culture as the long apology for everything we were not allowed to do. I have never found that account convincing, and neither, eventually, did Lacan. His revision in the seventh seminar is a bit strange but he goes on to say that sublimation "raises an object to the dignity of the Thing." It is the one vicissitude of the drive that achieves satisfaction *without* repression. The painter at her canvas is not making do. She is getting something, directly, that the rest of us merely circle around. Sublimation is therefore kind of the goal not a re-rerouting of something else.

Karl Kraus once said in some translation: *pity the man who, wanting only the foot, must nevertheless make do with the woman.* 

Conventional morality pities the fetishist for the narrowness of his desire; ‘how could he confuse the part for the whole, poor soul’. To Kraus it is the fetishist who is the one man in the room who knows exactly what he wants. Pity the rest. His tragedy is sociological not psychological insofar as the the social world is not organised to deliver feet à la carte, so he must willy nilly accept the entire package, the whole person with her opinions and her family and her Tuesday moods, in order to obtain the one thing he actually desires. The rest of us, meanwhile, want “the woman” in some vague gestural way and could not under interrogation specify what in particular we are after. The pervert has the better epistemic position. Advertising does not sell feet but sells "the woman" as an indeterminate package, the lifestyle, the *more*, because a desire that knew its precise object could be satisfied and would then stop spending. The market needs one be uncastrated in fantasy and convinced that totality is one purchase away, while keeping one castrated in fact. Psychoanalysis is in this sense bad for business. A person who has genuinely accepted his castration is a terrible customer.

So how does one go on to be free? The popular image of freedom is the open menu of maximal options, no commitments, every door propped ajar. Anyone who has spent forty minutes scrolling a streaming service knows how that picture ends, namely in a paralysis tantamount to servitude. The infinite menu is the fantasy of the uncastrated subject, and it produces, reliably, a person who chooses nothing and resents everything. Real freedom seems to work the other way around. The sonneteer accepts fourteen lines and finds in this constraint a precision unavailable to the writer of formless sprawl. Odysseus has himself bound to the mast and is, lashed there, the only free man on the ship, since his crew with their waxed ears merely cannot hear the sirens while he hears them and survives the hearing. The constraint is therefore a condition for freedom.

Pity rather the man who wants everything. He will make do with nothing.
