Should We Kill Predators?

In this post, I’ll argue:

  1. Nonhuman animals possess moral value.
  2. Their moral value is significant in contexts beyond human exploitation, including their suffering as prey in natural ecosystems.

The Argument from Species Overlap

Let’s begin with the foundational question: Do animals matter morally?

 

Opponents of animal moral value often argue that traits like advanced rationality, self-awareness, or moral agency are necessary for moral consideration. Since animals lack these, they fall outside our ethical circle.

 

But this logic fails when applied consistently. It animals do not have moral value based on lacking on or more of these traits, then so would marginal cases of humans. And we do not want to say that they also lack moral value in virtue of lacking those traits. These humans include, for example, infants, the severely cognitively disabled, or individuals with advanced dementia. These groups lack the same traits used to exclude animals, yet we (rightly) deem it abhorrent to deny them moral value. 

 

Consider:

  • P1: If moral value depends on trait X (e.g., rationality), then any being lacking X lacks moral value.
  • P2: Marginal humans lack X.
  • C: Therefore, marginal humans lack moral value.

Yet almost no one accepts that conclusion. To resolve this, we have two options:

  1. Bite the bullet: Deny moral value to marginal humans.
  2. Expand the moral circle: Acknowledge that trait-based thresholds are flawed, and grant moral value to animals.

 

Since the first option is morally repugnant—and likely unthinkable to most readers, the second option seems to be the one we must go for.

 

Having established that trait-based thresholds fail to exclude animals without also excluding marginal humans, critics might retreat to a simpler symmetry-breaker: “But humans are special because they’re human.” In other words, the morally relevant differences between humans and nonhuman animals that would justify treating them differently in terms of mass killing for consumption is not ‘intelligence’ or ‘rationality’, but that they are ‘humans’. ‘Humanity’ is the relevant trait that breaks the symmetry. After all, the senile, infants, mentally disabled, and whatever other marginal cases of humans, all share the similar trait of being human.

 

This objection typically manifests in two forms—one biological, the other tribal.

1. Species Membership: DNA

The Claim: “Humanity” is defined by membership in Homo sapiens—i.e., beings whose DNA traces back to Homo erectus. Thus, even marginal humans deserve moral consideration, while animals do not.

 

The Rebuttal: Genealogy is morally irrelevant. Consider a thought experiment where archaeologists discover Homo fictus, a species identical to humans in intelligence, sentience, and social bonds, but with DNA tracing to a different hominin lineage. Under the species-membership criterion, farming, experimenting on, or slaughtering Homo fictus would be morally permissible—a conclusion so repugnant it collapses the premise. If we wouldn’t exploit Homo fictus, we cannot justify exploiting pigs, cows, or chickens based on lineage alone.

2. In-Group Membership: Tribal

The Claim: “Human” signifies in-group loyalty. We prioritise humans for the same reason families prioritise kin: moral value derives from affiliation, not objective traits.

 

The Rebuttal: This logic justifies any arbitrary in-group preference. For example, slaveholders claimed moral superiority over enslaved people based on race; patriarchs denied women rights based on sex. In-group bias, when universalised, licenses oppression. As A.W.F. Edwards noted, genetic differences among humans are non-trivial. If we premise moral equality on biological similarity, any discovered dissimilarity (e.g., IQ variance, disease susceptibility) could justify hierarchy. This is not just speculative, it has been, and still is, a playbook used to rationalise eugenics and caste systems.

 

If neither traits nor tribe nor taxonomy can justify excluding animals, what remains? I argue sentience—the capacity to experience to have a subjective experience of your surroundings and feel pleasure and suffering. Every proposed “cutoff” for moral consideration—intelligence, language, moral agency—collapses under the weight of marginal human cases. But sentience is both necessary and sufficient for moral relevance. To experience suffering, pleasure, or desire is to have interests that can be harmed or fulfilled. If we value minimising harm (as nearly all ethical systems claim), sentience becomes the bedrock of moral concern.

 

Does this mean that Non-Human Animals Have Equal Moral Value to Humans?

 

Moral value need not be binary or identically weighted to demand ethical action. A dog’s suffering need not “equal” a human’s to warrant concern. What matters is whether the suffering exists and whether we can mitigate it without greater cost. Also: Suppose we do grant human animals greater moral weight than non-human animals. Even so, inflicting vastly more suffering on animals (e.g., slaughtering 80 billion land animals annually) would still require a proportionally stronger justification. Does “taste pleasure” outweigh trillions of hours of suffering? The math rarely holds.

 

To recap the argument: 

  • P1: If an ethical stance grants moral value to humans who lack trait X (e.g., rationality), it must grant moral value to animals who also lack X.
  • P2: Humans like infants or the severely disabled lack X but retain moral value.
  • C: Therefore, animals lacking X must also retain moral value.

To reject the conclusion, one must either:

  1. Deny moral value to marginal humans (a repugnant option)
  2. Identify a morally relevant trait that marginal humans possess and animals lack.

 

No such trait survives scrutiny. Appeal to special traits fail’; appeal to ‘Humanity’ fails; appeals to ‘potential’ fail (e.g., infants will develop rationality crumble when applied to permanently disabled humans).

 

The default presumption is that, when harm is inflicted, the burden of proof lies with the perpetrator, not the victim. To justify treating animals worse than humans, you must:

  1. Identify a trait T that distinguishes the groups,
  2. Show why T is morally relevant to the specific harm being inflicted.
     

So let’s say you want to kill pigs for food, and you justify this by appealing to a trait: “Pigs lack self-awareness!”. Does lacking self-awareness justify slaughter? If so, then humans in persistent vegetative states (who also lack self-awareness) could be farmed. Absent a coherent T, the differential treatment is arbitrary.

 

If sentience grounds moral value, and no defensible symmetry-breaker exists, we face an obligation to reduce suffering systematically. This extends beyond human exploitation, i.e., even when humans aren’t the cause.

Wild Animal Predation

The argument for animal moral value becomes thornier when applied to ‘natural’ suffering. If we accept that sentience grounds moral concern, should we intervene to prevent predation, disease, or starvation in the wild? To test our consistency, let’s examine a hypothetical:

 

Scenario A (Xenomorphs):

A xenomorph species evolved to prey on humans. You’re in a helicopter with a sniper rifle, poised to kill one before it hunts a human. Your likely intuition is: shoot first, ask questions later. Most would prioritise human life over xenomorph autonomy or ecological balance.

 

Scenario B (Lions):

A lion stalks a gazelle. You have the means to kill the lion and spare the gazelle. Your likely intuition is: Don’t shoot! 

 

Why do we treat these cases differently when both involve sentient beings harming others for survival? Some may cite the ‘ecological’ defence: Predation is morally neutral because it’s part of the “natural order” or ecosystem stability, and intervening in predation would cause cascading harm (e.g., overpopulation, starvation). 

 

The problem with this line of reasoning is that, in Scenario A, few consider the xenomorph’s ecological role—yet in Scenario B, ecological impact dominates. This inconsistency is an anthropocentric scope neglect: we arbitrarily weigh consequences only when nonhumans are involved. Also, even if ecosystems did matter consistently through both scenarios, humans are by far the species most responsible for environmental degradation. Prioritising xenomorphs over humans would be, ecologically speaking, the best outcome.

 

As with factory farming, predation ethics demand a morally relevant trait distinguishing human and animal victims. Yet:

  • Sentience: Both humans and gazelles experience terror and pain.
  • Ecological Value: Humans are net ecological harms; gazelles are neutral or beneficial.
  • Agency: Predators (xenomorphs, lions) lack moral agency.

No trait justifies the asymmetry in concern without invoking circular logic.

 

Trophic Ethics: Odd-order predator culling

The concept of "odd-order killing" is a theoretical framework I came across in random discord servers, which, if I understand it correctly, aims to minimise harm in ecosystems by targeting predators at specific trophic levels. The idea hinges on classifying predators by their position in a simplified linear food chain and targeting those in "odd orders" to indirectly protect herbivores. Imagine a linear, five-step food chain:

 

grass → grasshopper (herbivore) → frog → snake → eagle

 

Each level is numbered sequentially, starting with the herbivore as position (a). The predators are then labeled based on whom they prey upon:

Odd-order predators occupy positions 1, 3, 5... in the chain. In this example:

  • Frog (1): Directly preys on the herbivore (grasshopper).
  • Eagle (3): Preys on the snake (2), which itself preys on the frog (1).

These odd-order predators are critical targets because their removal can theoretically reduce herbivore predation through two pathways. So let’s say we want to minimise harm to grasshoppers by disrupting predation, we can either go with:

1. Direct Intervention (Kill Frog)

Eliminating the frog (1) removes the immediate predator of grasshoppers. The result is that grasshoppers survive, but frogs no longer control their population.

2. Indirect Intervention (Kill Eagle)

Killing the eagle (3) stops it from preying on snakes (2). Snakes (2) increase in number. More snakes eat more frogs (1). Fewer frogs mean fewer grasshoppers are eaten. The result is that grasshoppers survive due to snake-mediated frog suppression.

 

On the other hand, the snake (2) is an even-order predator (position 2), so cannot be killed directly. Removing it would allow frog (1) populations to grow unchecked and lead to more grasshoppers being eaten. At any rate, while the pragmatic reality may justify differential treatment of humans and nonhumans when it comes to predation, it clearly does not do so in the context of humans actively breeding billions of animals into existence and slaughtering them en mass.

 

The problem with this solution is that most predators occupy multiple trophic roles. A snake (2) might eat both frogs (1) and grasshoppers (a). Also, removing a predator could very easily destabilise ecosystems (e.g., overpopulation of prey, cascading trophic effects).
